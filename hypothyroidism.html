A Note from Dr. Bray: Understanding Your Thyroid

Welcome. At Archangel Michael Health, where we practice both Internal Medicine and Integrative Medicine, our philosophy is to identify and address the root causes of health problems early. We use common-sense, practical, and effective lifestyle interventions alongside conventional treatments.

This guide to hypothyroidism is written with that philosophy in mind. This condition is far more than a simple lab value; it's a system-wide issue influenced by diet, stress, environment, and immune health. The thyroid gland, a small butterfly-shaped organ in your neck, is your body's master metabolic regulator. It controls energy production, body temperature, and the function of nearly every system in your body. When it's not functioning optimally, the effects can be profound.

Our goal is to empower you with the knowledge to be an active participant in your health, blending the best of standard-of-care medicine with evidence-based lifestyle strategies to help you restore your energy and vitality.

What is Hypothyroidism?

Hypothyroidism means your thyroid gland is not producing enough thyroid hormone. This deficiency slows down your body's metabolic processes. It is a common condition, affecting approximately 1 in 8 women during their lifetime.

To monitor this, we look at how the brain (pituitary gland) communicates with the thyroid using Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH). If the thyroid is struggling, the pituitary releases more TSHâ€”it "yells louder"â€”to try and boost production.

The Sensitivity of TSH

TSH is the most sensitive marker for thyroid dysfunction because the relationship is logarithmic: a small, 2-fold change in thyroid hormone (T4) results in a massive 100-fold change in TSH.

Overt Hypothyroidism

The thyroid cannot keep up with demand. Lab tests show:

  • High TSH (The brain is yelling loudly)
  • Low T4 (Thyroid hormone levels have dropped)

Symptoms are usually present, and treatment is required.

Subclinical Hypothyroidism (SCH)

The "early-warning" stage. The thyroid is struggling but managing. Lab tests show:

  • High TSH (Typically 4.5â€“10.0 mIU/L)
  • Normal T4 (Hormone levels are still adequate)

This is a key stage where early interventions can be effective. About 2-5% of cases progress to overt hypothyroidism annually.

Who Is at Risk?

Several factors significantly increase the risk of developing hypothyroidism.

Gender: Women are 8-10x more likely than men Age: Prevalence increases progressively with age Family History: 9x increased risk with a first-degree relative Autoimmune History: Increased risk if you have Type 1 Diabetes, RA, or Celiac Disease Pregnancy and Postpartum Period Previous Thyroid Treatments (Surgery or Radiation) Certain Medications (e.g., Lithium, Amiodarone)

The Symptom Spectrum

Because thyroid hormones affect every cell, the symptoms of an underactive thyroid are broad and often develop slowly, mimicking other conditions or normal aging.

Symptoms of Hypothyroidism (Underactive or Under-treated)

Fatigue, exhaustion, or sluggishness
Unintentional weight gain or difficulty losing weight
Sensitivity to cold (Cold intolerance)
Dry, coarse, or pale skin; sometimes yellowing (carotenemia)
Thinning hair (including eyebrows) or brittle nails
Constipation and digestive sluggishness
Depression, low mood, or anxiety
"Brain fog," difficulty concentrating, or poor memory
Heavy, frequent, or irregular menstrual periods; Infertility
Muscle aches, joint stiffness, or weakness
Slowed heart rate; Elevated cholesterol
Puffy face or swelling of the thyroid (goiter)

The Other Side: Recognizing Over-Treatment

The goal of therapy is balance. Taking too much thyroid medication can push your body into a state of hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid), which carries long-term risks, including atrial fibrillation (irregular heart rhythm) and osteoporosis (bone loss). Monitor these symptoms closely:

Nervousness, anxiety, or irritability
Insomnia or difficulty sleeping
Increased sweating or feeling hot
Shaky hands or tremors
A racing heart or palpitations
Frequent bowel movements or diarrhea
Weight loss despite a good appetite

Why Does This Happen? The Root Causes

Understanding the cause is the most important step in developing a comprehensive treatment plan.

The Autoimmune Driver: Hashimoto's Disease

In the United States and other iodine-sufficient countries, Hashimoto's thyroiditis causes up to 90% of primary hypothyroidism cases.

It is critical to understand that Hashimoto's is primarily a disease of the immune system. The immune system mistakenly produces antibodies (anti-TPO and anti-Tg) that attack the thyroid tissue, causing inflammation and slowly reducing its function over time.

Other Common Causes

  • Iatrogenic (Medically-Caused): The second-leading cause. Includes thyroid surgery (thyroidectomy) or radioactive iodine (RAI) treatment. 80-90% of people treated with RAI eventually develop hypothyroidism.
  • Iodine Imbalance: While iodine deficiency is the leading cause globally, in the U.S., excess iodine (e.g., from high-dose kelp supplements) can actually trigger or worsen hypothyroidism.

The Integrative View: Underlying Triggers

From an integrative perspective, especially with Hashimoto's, we ask: Why is the immune system dysfunctional? We look deeper at systemic triggers:

  • Chronic Stress: Elevates cortisol, which can blunt thyroid function and trigger autoimmune flare-ups.
  • Gut Health and Food Sensitivities: Over 70% of the immune system is in the gut. "Leaky gut" (intestinal permeability) and food sensitivities (especially gluten and dairy) are strongly linked to autoimmune processes.
  • Environmental Toxins: Exposure to certain chemicals can disrupt thyroid signaling.

The Thyroid System Explained: T4, T3, and Conversion

Understanding the hormones helps you understand your treatment. The process starts in the brain, which signals the pituitary gland to release TSH. TSH tells the thyroid gland to produce its hormones.

T4 (Thyroxine) - The "Storage" Hormone

The main hormone your thyroid produces. T4 is relatively inactive; it's a "storage" hormone with a 7-10 day half-life, waiting to be activated.

T3 (Triiodothyronine) - The "Active" Hormone

The "active" hormone that "presses the gas pedal" for your metabolism. It is significantly more potent than T4. This hormone makes you feel warm, energetic, and clear-headed.

The Crucial Step: T4-to-T3 Conversion

Here is a critical concept: The vast majority of active T3 is not made in the thyroid gland. It is made in peripheral tissues (primarily the liver and gut) by converting T4 into T3 using a deiodinase enzyme.

Your ability to convert T4 to T3 is just as important as your thyroid's ability to produce T4.

Reverse T3 (rT3) - The "Emergency Brakes"

In times of stress, illness, injury, or famine (severe dieting), the body conserves energy. It takes T4 and converts it into Reverse T3 (rT3), an inactive form.

Think of T3 as the gas pedal and rT3 as the emergency brake. High rT3 leads to "functional hypothyroidism"â€”your TSH and T4 may look normal, but you feel hypothyroid because you lack active T3.

Factors That Cause Poor Conversion (High rT3)

  • Chronic stress and elevated cortisol levels
  • Severe illness or injury
  • Calorie restriction and rapid weight loss
  • Systemic inflammation
  • Nutritional deficiencies (Selenium, Zinc, Iron)
  • Overtraining (excessive high-intensity exercise)

Our Diagnostic Approach: Looking Beyond TSH

Because of this complexity, a comprehensive diagnostic panel must look beyond just the TSH.

The Full Thyroid Panel

  • TSH: The pituitary messenger hormone. The most sensitive test.
  • Free T4 (FT4): The amount of available storage hormone.
  • Free T3 (FT3): The amount of available active hormone. (Often remains normal until hypothyroidism is severe).
  • Thyroid Antibodies (TPO Ab and Tg Ab): TPO antibodies appear in 90% of Hashimoto's cases. Positive antibodies indicate a higher risk of progression and tell us we must focus on calming the immune system.
  • Reverse T3 (rT3): Assessed in persistent cases to check for conversion issues.

Optimal vs. Reference Ranges

The "reference ranges" on lab reports (e.g., TSH 0.4â€“4.5 mIU/L) are statistical averages of the general population. However, the median TSH in healthy populations clusters around 1.0â€“1.5 mIU/L. We focus on the "optimal" or "functional" ranges where healthy, asymptomatic people typically fall.

Optimal (Functional) Ranges

  • TSH: 1.0 - 2.0 mIU/L (We are suspicious of TSH > 2.5 if symptomatic)
  • Free T4: Upper 1/3rd of the reference range (e.g., 1.4 - 1.8 ng/dL)
  • Free T3: Upper 1/2 of the reference range (e.g., 3.4 - 4.2 pg/mL)

CRITICAL NOTE: Biotin Interference

High-dose biotin (Vitamin B7), common in hair/nail supplements, severely interferes with thyroid lab assays. It can cause falsely low TSH and falsely high T4/T3 levels, leading to incorrect diagnosis.

You MUST stop all biotin supplements for at least 3 to 7 days (72 hours minimum) before having your blood drawn for thyroid tests.

Pregnancy and Hypothyroidism

Thyroid management during pregnancy is critical for maternal and fetal health. Fetal brain development depends on maternal thyroid hormone.

Action Steps for Pregnancy

  1. Pre-conception Planning: Optimize thyroid levels before getting pregnant.
  2. Immediate Action if Pregnant: If you are already taking levothyroxine, increase your dose by 25-30% immediately upon confirmation of pregnancy (e.g., take two extra tablets weekly) and contact your healthcare provider for monitoring. Thyroid hormone requirements increase significantly during pregnancy.
  3. Monitoring: Pregnancy requires tighter TSH control using trimester-specific ranges:
    • First Trimester: Upper limit 2.5 mIU/L
    • Second Trimester: Upper limit 3.0 mIU/L
    • Third Trimester: Upper limit 3.5 mIU/L

Postpartum Thyroiditis

The postpartum period is a vulnerable time. Postpartum thyroiditis affects 5-11% of women, typically appearing 8-20 weeks after delivery. Up to 50% of affected women may develop permanent hypothyroidism.

Pharmaceutical Treatment Options

The standard (conventional) treatment is daily thyroid hormone replacement. The goal is restoring normal TSH levels and resolving symptoms. There is no "one-size-fits-all" medication.

Standard Care: T4-Only (Levothyroxine)

Levothyroxine (synthetic T4) is the gold standard treatment. It relies on your body's ability to convert T4 to T3. Most patients feel well on this alone.

Initial Dosing Guidelines

  • Healthy adults under 60: Typically start with a full replacement dose (calculated around 1.6 mcg per kilogram of body weight daily).
  • Adults over 60 or those with heart disease: Start lower (25-50 mcg daily) and increase gradually every 3-4 weeks to avoid stressing the cardiovascular system.

Formulations

  • Generics/Synthroid (Tablets): Common options. May contain various fillers (lactose, corn starch) and dyes.
  • Tirosint (Softgel): A "cleaner" T4 option (contains only levothyroxine, glycerin, gelatin, and water). Often better tolerated by those with sensitivities or absorption issues.
  • Tirosint-SOL (Liquid): A liquid formulation useful for severe malabsorption issues; may be taken just 15 minutes before eating.

Brand vs. Generic: The Need for Consistency

While the FDA considers generics bioequivalent to brand names, inactive ingredients (fillers, dyes) vary between manufacturers. Thyroid medication has a "narrow therapeutic index," meaning small changes can matter clinically.

The American Thyroid Association (ATA) advises maintaining the same preparationâ€”either a specific brand OR the same generic manufacturerâ€”to avoid fluctuations. If your pharmacy switches your generic manufacturer, recheck TSH in 4-6 weeks.

Combination Therapies (T4 and T3)

About 5-10% of patients still have symptoms despite normal TSH on T4-only therapy, often due to poor T4-to-T3 conversion. While evidence does not support routine use, and major medical societies consider it experimental, a trial may be considered in these cases.

Natural Desiccated Thyroid (NDT)

Derived from porcine thyroid extract (e.g., Armour, Nature-Throid). Contains both T4 and T3.

  • Ratio: NDT has a T3-heavy ratio (approx. 4:1) compared to human physiology (approx. 14:1). This T3 boost is why many "poor converters" report feeling better.
  • Considerations: Hormone content can sometimes vary, making the dose less predictable than synthetic options.

Synthetic Combination

Adding synthetic T3 (Liothyronine/Cytomel) to a T4 medication. If a trial shows no improvement after 3 months, the T3 should be discontinued.

Practical Medication Management: Ensuring Success

Taking your medication correctly is crucial. Variable absorption is the number one enemy of stable thyroid levels.

Timing and Absorption

Levothyroxine absorption is finicky. Absorption is maximized (60-80%) when fasting, but drops significantly (to 40-64%) when taken with food.

How and When to Take Your Medication

  1. Empty Stomach: Take levothyroxine first thing in the morning with a full glass of water, on an empty stomach.
  2. Wait Before Eating: Wait at least 60 minutes before eating breakfast.
  3. Wait Before Coffee: Coffee (especially espresso) reduces absorption by 29-36%. Wait at least 60 minutes before drinking coffee.
  4. Alternative Timing: Take at bedtime, provided it is at least 3-4 hours after your last meal. Some studies suggest this may provide slightly better absorption.
  5. Consistency is Key: Pick one method and be consistent every single day.
  6. Monitoring: Check TSH every 4-8 weeks after starting medication or changing the dose until stable, then every 6-12 months.

ðŸš« Absorption Blockers: 4-Hour Rule

The following substances bind the hormone and prevent absorption. Wait at least 4 hours after your thyroid pill before taking these:

  • Supplements: Calcium (in any form), Iron (including multivitamins with iron), Magnesium, Zinc.
  • Foods: High-fiber meals, Soy products (soy milk, soy protein), Walnuts.
  • Medications: Aluminum-containing Antacids (like Tums), Proton Pump Inhibitors (e.g., omeprazole), Bile acid sequestrants.

Gastrointestinal Conditions and Absorption

Gut health critically affects absorption. Conditions like Celiac disease, atrophic gastritis, H. pylori infection, lactose intolerance, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), and SIBO can impair levothyroxine absorption, potentially requiring higher doses or alternative formulations (like Tirosint/Tirosint-SOL).

Lifestyle as Medicine: Nutrition Strategy

Diet cannot replace thyroid hormone, but it is essential for supporting thyroid function, reducing inflammation, and addressing the root causes of Hashimoto's. Emphasize a nutrient-dense, anti-inflammatory diet (e.g., Mediterranean style), focusing on whole foods and minimizing sugar and processed items.

Foods to Be Cautious With

The Autoimmune Connection: Gluten & Dairy

If you have Hashimoto's (positive antibodies), addressing food sensitivities is critical.

  • Gluten and Celiac Disease: Celiac disease occurs in 3.5-9.3% of people with Hashimoto's (compared to 1% of the general population). Molecular mimicry between gluten proteins and thyroid tissue may trigger immune responses.
  • Testing Requirement: All people with Hashimoto's should be screened for Celiac disease before trying a gluten-free diet, as testing becomes inaccurate once gluten is removed.
  • Gluten Sensitivity: Even without Celiac disease, some studies show a gluten-free diet may reduce thyroid antibodies by 15-30% over 6 months. If you have persistent symptoms, consider a strict 6-month trial.

The "Kale Myth": Goitrogens

Goitrogenic foods (raw cruciferous vegetables like broccoli, kale, cabbage) contain glucosinolates that can interfere with iodine uptake, but only when consumed in extraordinarily large, raw quantities (e.g., 15+ cups of raw bok choy daily) combined with iodine deficiency.

  • Common-Sense Solution: Cooking destroys approximately 90% of goitrogens. Steaming, boiling, or sautÃ©ing these vegetables makes them safe for regular consumption (1-2 cups daily).
  • Soy: Fine in moderation, but consume it at least 4 hours away from your thyroid medication as it impairs absorption.

Integrative Strategies: Lifestyle and Environment

Exercise: Balancing Metabolism

Exercise addresses many symptoms: combating slow metabolism, elevating mood, and boosting energy. However, balance is key.

Aerobic Foundation

Aim for 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity weekly (walking, swimming, cycling). Consistency is crucial.

Strength Training

Two sessions weekly builds lean muscle mass, which increases resting metabolic rate. Focus on functional, compound movements.

Avoid Overtraining

Excessive high-intensity exercise increases cortisol, which suppresses TSH and blocks T4-to-T3 conversion, worsening hypothyroid symptoms. Train at intensities appropriate for your current fitness level.

Stress Management and the Cortisol Connection

Chronic stress and thyroid dysfunction create a vicious cycle. The brain axes that control stress (HPA) and thyroid (HPT) are intimately connected.

  • Hormonally: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which inhibits TSH production and blocks conversion of T4 to active T3.
  • Immunologically: Stress increases inflammation and worsens autoimmune conditions like Hashimoto's.

Effective stress managementâ€”daily meditation, mindfulness, yoga, or deep breathingâ€”is essential for optimal thyroid function.

Sleep Optimization

Hypothyroidism disrupts sleep, and poor sleep worsens thyroid function. Hypothyroidism reduces slow-wave "refreshing" sleep and increases sleep latency (difficulty falling asleep).

Sleep Apnea Risk

Hypothyroidism significantly increases the risk for obstructive sleep apnea (affecting 9-24% of hypothyroid patients). If you snore loudly or experience excessive daytime sleepiness, discuss sleep apnea evaluation.

Prioritize 7-9 hours (often 8 hours is needed) of quality sleep nightly with consistent sleep hygiene (dark room, cool temperature, no screens before bed).

Reducing Your Toxic Load: Endocrine Disruptors

Your thyroid is sensitive to environmental chemicals that disrupt endocrine function. Iodine belongs to the "halide" family; other halides and chemicals can compete with iodine uptake or block thyroid hormone action.

Major Thyroid-Disrupting Chemicals

  • BPA (and BPS/BPF): Found in food can linings, plastic bottles, and thermal receipts. Antagonizes T3 receptors.
  • Phthalates: Found in fragrances, vinyl flooring, food packaging, and PVC plastics (recycling code #3). Blocks thyroid hormone action.
  • Perchlorate: Contaminates drinking water and soil. Competitively inhibits iodine uptake.
  • Fluoride: Found in drinking water and toothpaste. Interferes with iodine (as another halogen). High levels are associated with increased TSH.
  • Others: Pesticides, flame retardants (PBDEs), and PFAS (non-stick cookware).

Practical Exposure Reduction Strategies

  • Water Quality: Install a Reverse Osmosis (RO) filtration system. RO is most effective at removing perchlorate, pesticides, fluoride, and heavy metals. Standard carbon/pitcher filters are insufficient.
  • Food Choices: Store food in glass or stainless steel instead of plastic. Buy organic produce when possible.
  • Household: Replace non-stick cookware with stainless steel, cast iron, or ceramic. Vacuum frequently with a HEPA filter.
  • Personal Care: Choose "fragrance-free" products (fragrance often hides phthalates). Use the Environmental Working Group (EWG) database to check product safety.
