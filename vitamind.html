Vitamin D Deficiency & Insufficiency

Patient Guide: Optimization, Immunity & Bone Health

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

Vitamin D is frequently misunderstood as a simple nutrient. It is, in fact, a powerful secosteroid hormone with receptors found in nearly every tissue of the human body, from your white blood cells to your brain and heart. While conventional medicine has historically focused on Vitamin D solely for the prevention of rickets and bone fractures, at Archangel Michael Health we view optimal Vitamin D levels as a cornerstone of preventative medicine. It is essential for modulating the immune system, regulating inflammation, supporting mental health, and maintaining metabolic stability.

The following guide is designed for the sophisticated patient who wishes to understand the root causes of their condition and how to safely optimize their physiology. We prioritize natural production through sun exposure whenever possible, followed by bio-identical supplementation when necessary.

Overview: The Pleiotropic Hormone

Vitamin D regulates the expression of hundreds, potentially thousands, of genes. Its primary pathway begins in the skin, where UVB radiation converts cholesterol into a precursor that is eventually activated by the liver and kidneys. This active hormone, calcitriol, does not just build bone; it acts as a "key" that unlocks genetic instructions for immune defense, insulin sensitivity, and cell differentiation.

High-Risk Groups & Conditions

While indoor living makes nearly everyone susceptible, certain medical conditions and biological factors significantly elevate the risk of severe deficiency. Patients in these categories require aggressive monitoring and often higher doses of Vitamin D to maintain optimal levels.

1. Geographic & Environmental Risk

Latitude: Solar angle determines UVB intensity. If you live north of the 35th parallel (roughly the line from Los Angeles to Atlanta), the sun's angle is too low during winter months to stimulate Vitamin D production, regardless of how much time you spend outside.

2. Medical Conditions & Medications

  • Malabsorption Syndromes: Conditions that impair fat absorption prevent Vitamin D uptake. These include Celiac disease, Crohn’s disease, Ulcerative Colitis, Cystic Fibrosis, and exocrine pancreatic insufficiency.
  • Bariatric Surgery: Gastric bypass procedures often bypass the part of the small intestine where Vitamin D is best absorbed.
  • Liver & Kidney Disease: These organs are responsible for activating Vitamin D. Chronic kidney disease (CKD) or liver failure impairs the conversion to the active hormone.
  • Medications: Several drugs accelerate the breakdown of Vitamin D, including corticosteroids (Prednisone), anticonvulsants (Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Phenobarbital), and certain HIV medications.

3. Biological Factors

  • Dark Skin Pigmentation: Melanin acts as a natural sunscreen. Individuals with darker skin (Fitzpatrick types IV–VI) require 3 to 10 times longer sun exposure to generate the same amount of Vitamin D as those with lighter skin.
  • Obesity: Vitamin D is fat-soluble and gets sequestered (trapped) in adipose tissue. Patients with a higher BMI often need 2–3 times the standard dose to raise blood levels.
  • Age > 65: As we age, our skin becomes thinner and less efficient at synthesizing Vitamin D from sunlight, producing only about 25% as much as a young adult under the same conditions.

Symptoms and Clinical Signs

Deficiency is often called a "silent epidemic" because symptoms can be nonspecific and gradual. However, detailed history taking often reveals a pattern of suboptimal physiology.

  • Musculoskeletal: Deep, aching bone pain (often in the low back, pelvis, or legs), generalized muscle weakness, proximal muscle atrophy, and increased sensitivity to pain (sometimes misdiagnosed as fibromyalgia).
  • Immune Dysfunction: Frequent respiratory infections (colds, flu), prolonged recovery from illness, or a history of autoimmune flares (e.g., Hashimoto's, Rheumatoid Arthritis, MS).
  • Neuropsychiatric: Fatigue that does not resolve with sleep, "brain fog," seasonal affective disorder (SAD), and depression. Vitamin D receptors in the brain regulate neurotransmitters like serotonin.
Red flags: In children, severe deficiency causes rickets (bowing of legs). In adults, it leads to osteomalacia (soft bones) and a significantly increased risk of falls and hip fractures. Sudden, severe bone pain or unprovoked fractures warrant immediate medical imaging and evaluation.

Diagnosis and Reference Ranges

Diagnosis is confirmed via a simple blood test measuring 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D]. This is the storage form of the vitamin and the most accurate indicator of your status.

  • Deficiency (< 20 ng/mL): A state of medical urgency. At this level, the body often pulls calcium from the bones to maintain heart and nerve function, leading to bone loss.
  • Insufficiency (21–29 ng/mL): Common in standard care but suboptimal. While immediate bone collapse is unlikely, immune and metabolic functions are often compromised.
  • Optimal (40–60 ng/mL): The target for preventative medicine. Evidence suggests this range supports robust immune function, reduced inflammation, and better genomic regulation without risk of toxicity.

The AMH Optimization Plan & Calculator

Dr. Bray's Protocol: We treat the root cause first. Our goal is to restore levels to the 40–60 ng/mL range through a combination of safe sun exposure and high-quality supplementation, while ensuring cofactor sufficiency.

1. Sun Exposure ("Sun First" Approach)

My preference is always to recommend sun exposure as first-line treatment where safe and appropriate. The skin produces a sulfated form of Vitamin D that may have unique cardiovascular benefits not found in oral pills.

The Protocol: Expose a large surface area of skin (arms and legs) to midday sun (10:00 AM to 2:00 PM) for the duration indicated by the calculator (typically 10-20 minutes for lighter skin), 2–3 times per week. The goal is exposure, not burning. Use the "Shadow Rule": if your shadow is shorter than you, you are making Vitamin D. After this short "D-production" window, apply non-toxic sunscreen or wear protective clothing to prevent skin damage.

2. Dietary Modifications

While food alone is rarely enough to correct a deficiency, it is a supportive pillar. Prioritize wild-caught fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), cod liver oil, and pasture-raised egg yolks. Mushrooms exposed to UV light can also provide small amounts.

Supplements & Cofactors

When sun exposure is insufficient, supplementation is necessary. However, quality and form matter immensely. I specifically recommend Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol). I do not recommend Vitamin D2 (Ergocalciferol) due to its inferior absorption and shorter duration of action in the body.

The Critical Cofactors: Magnesium & K2

Taking high-dose Vitamin D without these cofactors can be ineffective or even counterproductive. Vitamin D increases calcium absorption; Vitamin K2 ensures that calcium goes to your bones and not your arteries. Magnesium powers the enzymes that make Vitamin D work.

Conventional vs. Integrative Care

In standard internal medicine, you may be prescribed a high-dose green capsule containing 50,000 IU of Vitamin D2 (Ergocalciferol) to be taken weekly.

My Perspective: I never recommend Ergocalciferol (D2). Research shows it is less effective at raising and maintaining blood levels compared to D3. Furthermore, intermittent massive dosing may not provide the stable blood levels needed for immune support. We prefer consistent, daily dosing of D3 (Cholecalciferol) to mimic natural physiological inputs.

However, if we identify severe deficiency (levels < 12 ng/mL), we may use a higher daily dose of D3 (e.g., 5,000 IU or 10,000 IU) for a limited time (6–8 weeks) to rapidly refill stores, always followed by a re-check of your labs.

Cost Considerations

  • Testing: Vitamin D testing is covered by most insurance plans if there is a diagnostic code (fatigue, bone pain, deficiency). Cash prices are typically reasonable ($40–$60).
  • Supplements: Vitamin D3 is one of the most cost-effective interventions in medicine. A year's supply of high-quality Jarrow D3 can cost less than a single co-pay for a specialist visit.
