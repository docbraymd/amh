Hemorrhoidal Disease

Patient Guide: Lifestyle, Integrative & Conventional Care

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

Hemorrhoids are often dismissed as a minor nuisance, but for many patients, they represent a significant quality-of-life issue. At Archangel Michael Health, we view hemorrhoidal disease not just as a local anatomy problem, but as a signal of broader physiological strain—often involving connective tissue integrity, vascular health, and digestive mechanics. This guide is designed to help you understand the root causes and manage the condition using a tiered approach that prioritizes lifestyle and integrative interventions before escalating to surgery.

On this page

1. Overview and key points

Contrary to popular belief, hemorrhoids are not simply "varicose veins" of the anus. They are normal anatomical structures known as "anal cushions" that everyone is born with. These cushions—composed of blood vessels, smooth muscle, and connective tissue—act as a "fine seal" (like a washer in a faucet) to prevent leakage of stool or gas. Disease occurs when the supporting tissues weaken, causing these cushions to slide downward (prolapse), swell, and bleed.

  • Key point: The goal of treatment is not to remove all hemorrhoidal tissue (which can lead to incontinence) but to return the tissue to its normal, non-symptomatic state.
  • Key point: Most cases are mechanical in nature, driven by straining and pressure. Therefore, mechanics (posture and stool consistency) must be the first line of defense.
  • Key point: While creams provide temporary relief, they do not fix the underlying sliding mechanism. True healing requires connective tissue support and vascular toning.

2. Symptoms and red flags

Symptoms vary significantly depending on whether the hemorrhoid originates above the "dentate line" (Internal) or below it (External).

Common symptoms

  • Painless Bright Red Bleeding (Internal): Because internal tissues lack pain nerves, the most common sign is bright red blood dripping into the bowl or on tissue, often without any pain.
  • Prolapse (Internal): A sensation of tissue bulging or "slipping out" during bowel movements. In early stages, it retracts on its own; in later stages, it may need to be pushed back manually.
  • Pain and Swelling (External): External hemorrhoids are covered by sensitive skin. When irritated, they cause itching, burning, and palpable lumps.
  • Thrombosis (Acute Pain): If a blood clot forms in an external hemorrhoid, it presents as a sudden, hard, blue/purple lump accompanied by severe, constant pain.

Urgent guidance (red flags)

Never assume rectal bleeding is just hemorrhoids. You must seek evaluation if you experience:

  • Dark, tarry, or maroon stool (indicating bleeding higher in the GI tract).
  • Change in bowel habits (pencil-thin stools, new constipation/diarrhea).
  • Unexplained weight loss or abdominal pain.
  • Severe pain accompanied by fever or chills (signs of abscess/infection).
  • Large volume blood loss leading to dizziness or lightheadedness.

3. Diagnosis and physiology

Clinical note: The "Dentate Line"

The anal canal is divided by the dentate line. Above this line, nerves are "visceral" (sensitive to stretch but not pain/touch). Below this line, nerves are "somatic" (highly sensitive to pain). This explains why we can band internal hemorrhoids painlessly in the office, but external hemorrhoids require anesthesia for removal.

Diagnostic Approach
EVALUATION
Anoscopy
A small office scope used to directly visualize internal hemorrhoids, which are often too soft to be felt by a finger exam alone.
Rule Out
For patients over 45 or those with red flags, a colonoscopy is required to rule out colorectal cancer, polyps, or Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) before attributing bleeding to hemorrhoids.

4. Root causes and drivers

Treatment fails when we address the symptom but ignore the driver. The "Sliding Anal Canal Theory" suggests that the connective tissue anchors holding the cushions in place deteriorate due to chronic pressure.

The Mechanics of Defecation

Western vs. Traditional Toilets: The modern sitting toilet (90-degree hip angle) is anatomically flawed for defecation. It creates a "kink" in the rectum (via the puborectalis muscle), forcing you to strain to push stool out. In contrast, traditional "Turkish style" squat toilets require full hip flexion, which straightens this angle naturally.

Special Considerations for Women

For women, especially those who have had multiple children (multiparous), "constipation" may not be a simple lack of fiber. Childbirth can cause laxity in the pelvic floor tissues, leading to a rectocele (where the rectum bulges into the vagina) or rectal prolapse. This can trap stool and create a mechanical obstruction. If you feel like you cannot fully empty despite soft stool, you may have a motility or prolapse issue requiring specialized investigation rather than just more laxatives.

Dietary Factors

Ancestral human diets likely included 100g+ of fiber daily. The modern average is often less than 15g. We see this frequently in teenage boys or young men consuming diets heavy in meat, pasta, and bread with almost no plant roughage. This lack of bulk forces the body to generate excessive pressure to move stool, damaging the anal cushions over time.

5. The Improvement Plan

We utilize a tiered approach. Most patients (Grades I-II) can achieve complete symptom control with "The Foundation" alone.

The "Foundation" Protocol

1. Toilet Hygiene (The 2-Minute Rule): Do not linger. No phones, magazines, or scrolling. If a bowel movement does not occur in 2 minutes, get up and try again later.
2. Mechanical Advantage (Squatty Potty): Since most of us do not have access to Turkish squat toilets, we must mimic that position. Use a "Squatty Potty" or a simple footstool to elevate your knees above your hips while sitting. This relaxes the puborectalis muscle and straightens the anal canal, significantly reducing the need to strain.
3. Consistency: The goal is a soft, formed, bulky stool. It should not be liquid (which contains digestive enzymes that burn) and not rock hard (which tears tissue).

Fiber & Hydration
CRITICAL
The Goal
We aim for >30 grams of fiber daily. If you are currently eating <15g (common in "meat and potatoes" or fast-food diets), increase slowly to avoid bloating.
The Preferred Additive
Ground Flax Seed: This is our preferred fiber source. It provides an excellent mix of soluble and insoluble fiber, plus Omega-3 fatty acids which reduce inflammation. Add 1-2 tablespoons to smoothies, oatmeal, or yogurt daily.
Soluble Fiber
Absorbs water to form a gel. Softens hard stool and firms loose stool.
Sources: Oats, apples (pectin), beans, lentils, psyllium husk, chia seeds.
Insoluble Fiber
Adds "roughage" to speed up transit. Can be irritating if gut is very inflamed.
Sources: Wheat bran, whole grains, nuts, cauliflower, green beans, skins of fruits.
Hydration Rule
Fiber acts like a sponge. Without adequate water, fiber supplements will turn into "concrete" and worsen constipation. You must drink 1.5–2L (64oz) of water daily.

6. Conventional treatments

Conventional medications are excellent for managing acute flares while lifestyle changes take root.

Topical Medications (OTC & Rx)
STANDARD CARE
Corticosteroids
Examples: Hydrocortisone 1% (Anusol-HC, Preparation H Anti-Itch), Rx Hydrocortisone 2.5%.
Role: Potent anti-inflammatory. Reduces severe itching and swelling.
Warning: Do not use for more than 1 week. Prolonged use thins the anal skin (atrophy), making it prone to tearing and chronic dermatitis.
Vasoconstrictors
Examples: Phenylephrine (Preparation H Ointment/Suppositories).
Role: Actively shrinks swollen blood vessels to reduce congestion.
Warning: Caution in patients with high blood pressure, diabetes, or enlarged prostate (BPH), as it can be absorbed systematically.
Anesthetics
Examples: Pramoxine (Tucks Spray), Lidocaine (RectiCare).
Role: Numbs the nerve endings to provide temporary relief from pain and burning.
Note: Pramoxine is often preferred over "-caine" anesthetics as it carries a lower risk of allergic contact dermatitis.
Protectants
Examples: Cocoa Butter, Petrolatum (Vaseline), Zinc Oxide (Calmol).
Role: Forms a physical barrier over the irritated skin, preventing contact with stool and mucus. Safe for long-term use.

7. Integrative options

In Europe and Asia, "phlebotonics" (vein-toning agents) are standard medical therapy for hemorrhoids. In the US, these are available as high-quality supplements.

Sitz Bath
HYDROTHERAPY
What it is
A small plastic basin that fits over your toilet bowl (available at pharmacies or Amazon for ~$15). Alternatively, you can sit in a few inches of water in a bathtub.
How to use
Fill with warm (not scalding) water and dissolve a handful of Epsom salts. Soak for 10-15 minutes, 3 times a day, and immediately after bowel movements.
Advantages
Warm water relaxes the internal anal sphincter muscle. Since sphincter spasm is a major cause of pain in fissures and thrombosed hemorrhoids, this provides relief while improving blood flow for healing.
MPFF (Diosmin + Hesperidin)
PHLEBOTONIC
Rationale
Micronized Purified Flavonoid Fraction (MPFF). Clinically proven to reduce bleeding duration, pain, and recurrence risk by inhibiting inflammatory enzymes and improving venous tone.
Typical Use
Acute Flare: 1000mg 3x daily for 4 days, then 1000mg 2x daily for 3 days.
Maintenance: 500–1000mg daily for 2–3 months.
Cost
$20–$40/month. Look for "Diosmin/Hesperidin" formulas (often 90:10 ratio).
Topical Botanicals
SOOTHING
Witch Hazel
An astringent containing tannins. Causes tissues to contract/tighten, reducing "weeping" and itch. Use as cool compresses (pads) after bowel movements.
Aloe Vera
Vulnerary (wound healing). Pure gel can reduce pain and accelerate healing of fissures or irritated skin.

8. Surgical considerations

When lifestyle and integrative measures aren't enough, we escalate to procedures. While effective, surgery carries risks that should be weighed carefully.

Procedures & Surgery
INTERVENTIONAL
Rubber Band Ligation
The Gold Standard Office Procedure. A tiny band is placed around the base of an internal hemorrhoid. It cuts off blood supply, and the tissue falls off in 3–7 days. High success rate for Grade I-II with minimal downtime.
Thrombectomy
For acute external thrombosed hemorrhoids (painful blue lump). If caught within 72 hours, we can excise the clot under local anesthesia for immediate relief. After 72 hours, conservative care is usually better as the clot is already reabsorbing.
Surgical Risks
Surgery (Hemorrhoidectomy or Stapling) is effective for severe disease but carries significant downsides:
  • Severe Pain: Recovery is notoriously painful, often requiring 2-3 weeks off work.
  • Incontinence: Aggressive removal of tissue can compromise the "fine seal" function of the anal cushions, leading to leakage of gas or stool.
  • Anal Stenosis: Scarring from surgery can narrow the anal canal, making future bowel movements difficult and painful.
