Decoding Your Immune System

A Patient Guide to Autoimmunity Testing & Root Cause Solutions

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

At Archangel Michael Health, we believe you must be the CEO of your own health. Autoimmunity is rarely a sudden event; it is a spectrum that develops over years. Conventional medicine often waits for the "scream"—when organ damage is visible. We prefer to listen to the "whispers"—subtle shifts in blood markers that offer a window of opportunity. This guide bridges rigorous internal medicine with functional, root-cause strategies to help you interpret your testing and take action early.

On this page

1. The Philosophy: Whispers vs. Screams

Autoimmunity occurs when the immune system mistakenly targets healthy tissue. [cite_start]Research shows that autoantibodies (proteins that attack self-tissue) can appear in the blood up to 10 years before clinical symptoms like joint destruction or kidney failure occur. [cite: 5, 175]

  • Pre-Clinical Autoimmunity: This is the "golden window." You may have positive antibodies but no severe symptoms. [cite_start]This is not a "false positive" to be ignored; it is a signal that your immune tolerance is broken and needs repair. [cite: 339, 474]
  • The "Patient CEO": In our Direct Primary Care model, we remove the "middlemen" to give you the time and advanced data you need to make autonomous decisions. [cite_start]We treat problems early with common-sense lifestyle steps before expensive medications become necessary. [cite: 319, 324]

2. The Barometers: ESR & CRP

Before identifying what is being attacked, we must establish if there is a systemic fire. ESR and CRP are the two primary sentinels of inflammation, but they tell different stories.

ESR (Sed Rate)
CHRONIC MARKER
What it is
Measures how fast red blood cells settle. [cite_start]Inflammation causes them to clump (rouleaux) and fall faster. [cite: 348, 357]
The "Lag"
It reflects the "weather" of the last few weeks. [cite_start]It rises slowly and falls slowly. [cite: 363, 366]
Metabolic Note
Not just immunity—excess belly fat releases cytokines that raise ESR. [cite_start]A moderately high ESR without joint pain may be a metabolic weight signal, not Lupus. [cite: 381, 385]
Target
[cite_start]
Functional goal: Under 10 mm/hr (men) or 15 mm/hr (women). [cite: 374]
hs-CRP
ACUTE / SILENT MARKER
What it is
A direct measure of liver protein produced in response to IL-6. [cite_start]It rises within hours of an insult. [cite: 388, 391]
High-Sensitivity
[cite_start]
Unlike standard CRP (for infection), hs-CRP detects "silent" inflammation linked to heart disease and early autoimmunity. [cite: 405]
Risk Ranges
Optimal: < 0.5 mg/L
[cite_start]Integrative Warning: 1.0–3.0 mg/L (Suggests leaky gut, insulin resistance, or early reactivity). [cite: 409, 411]
Key Insight
[cite_start]
High CRP with Normal ESR usually means a very recent issue (infection) or purely metabolic inflammation (obesity). [cite: 418, 420]

3. The Gateway: ANA Testing

The Antinuclear Antibody (ANA) test is the entry point for investigation. It detects antibodies targeting the nucleus of your cells. A positive result is not a diagnosis of disease by itself; context is everything.

Understanding "Titers" and "Patterns"

The Titer (Quantity): A 1:40 titer is found in up to 30% of healthy people and is rarely significant. [cite_start]A titer of 1:160 or higher is more clinically weighty, appearing in only 5% of healthy individuals. [cite: 23, 24, 450]

The Pattern (Target): [cite_start]
Homogeneous: Common in Lupus (SLE). [cite: 26, 460] [cite_start]
Speckled: Non-specific; seen in Sjogren's, Lupus, or general environmental reactivity. [cite: 462] [cite_start]
Centromere: highly specific for Limited Scleroderma (CREST). [cite: 28, 466]

4. Specific Disease Markers

If the ANA is positive, we "reflex" to specific tests to identify the exact fingerprint of the immune attack.

Lupus (SLE) Markers
DIAGNOSTIC
Anti-dsDNA
Highly specific (97%) for Lupus. Unlike other markers, it fluctuates with disease activity. [cite_start]Rising levels often predict a kidney flare. [cite: 32, 507]
Anti-Sm (Smith)
The most specific antibody for Lupus. [cite_start]If positive, it is virtually diagnostic, though only 30% of patients have it. [cite: 44, 535]
Complement (C3/C4)
[cite_start]
Low levels: Suggest active "consumption" by immune complexes (Lupus flare). [cite: 107, 609] [cite_start]
High levels: Suggest insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome (fat cells produce C3). [cite: 616]
Rheumatoid Arthritis Markers
JOINT HEALTH
Rheumatoid Factor (RF)
The "Old Guard." [cite_start]Non-specific; can be elevated in Hep C, Sjogren's, or healthy aging. [cite: 68, 549]
Anti-CCP
The "Sniper." 96-98% specific for RA. Can appear 10-15 years before joint pain begins. [cite_start]Strongly linked to smoking and gum disease. [cite: 72, 555, 557]

Pregnancy Warning: Anti-SSA (Ro) & Anti-SSB (La)

These antibodies are markers for Sjogren's Syndrome (dry eye/mouth) and Lupus. Critically, Anti-SSA can cross the placenta and affect the fetal heart (heart block). [cite_start]Women of childbearing age with these markers require specialized monitoring during pregnancy. [cite: 86, 526]

5. Root Causes & Triggers

Genetics loads the gun, but environment pulls the trigger. In the "Pre-Clinical" phase, we look for these upstream causes to turn off the immune response.

  • Intestinal Permeability ("Leaky Gut"): The protein Zonulin regulates gut tightness. Gluten and dysbiosis trigger Zonulin release, allowing toxins into the blood. [cite_start]This triggers molecular mimicry where the immune system attacks self-tissue. [cite: 119, 630, 634]
  • Infections (EBV): The Epstein-Barr Virus (Mono) hides in B-cells. Its proteins look like human nuclear proteins. [cite_start]Chronic reactivation due to stress can drive ANA positivity. [cite: 481, 484]
  • Oral Health & The Lungs: Porphyromonas gingivalis (gum disease bacteria) and smoking both cause "citrullination" of proteins. [cite_start]This is the primary driver of Anti-CCP antibodies and Rheumatoid Arthritis. [cite: 564, 571]
  • Environmental Toxins: Mercury (from tuna/amalgams) and Silica dust are potent triggers for autoimmunity. [cite_start]Mercury levels correlate directly with ANA titers. [cite: 489, 592]

6. Integrative Solutions

The goal is to restore tolerance. We use a "treat-to-target" approach with lifestyle as the foundation, adding medications only when necessary to prevent organ damage.

Nutritional Strategy
LIFESTYLE
Mediterranean Diet
The gold standard for maintenance. High in Omega-3s and polyphenols. [cite_start]Proven to reduce RA pain and inflammatory markers. [cite: 128, 645]
Autoimmune Protocol (AIP)
For active flares. [cite_start]A temporary elimination of grains, dairy, nightshades, nuts, and eggs to "reset" the immune system, followed by systematic reintroduction. [cite: 133, 642]
Gluten-Free
[cite_start]
Essential for Celiac, but also highly effective for Sjogren's and Hashimotos due to molecular mimicry and barrier protection. [cite: 529]
Core Supplements
INTEGRATIVE
Vitamin D
Role: Teaches T-cells to be tolerant. Deficiency correlates with Lupus and MS. [cite_start]
Target: 40-60 ng/mL (higher than standard lab range). [cite: 144, 492]
Omega-3s (Fish Oil)
Role: Produces "resolvins" that actively turn off inflammation. [cite_start]
Dose: 2-4 grams of EPA/DHA daily has shown benefit in clinical trials. [cite: 150, 152]
Curcumin
Role: Potent anti-inflammatory that modulates cytokines. [cite_start]Use bioavailable forms for joint pain and systemic inflammation. [cite: 268, 296]

Medical Management

We are not anti-medication. When organs are threatened, drugs like Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil) are essential for Lupus to prevent permanent damage. [cite_start]The goal is the minimum effective dose alongside aggressive lifestyle changes. [cite: 187, 189]
