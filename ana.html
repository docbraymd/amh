Decoding Your Immune System

A Comprehensive Guide to Autoimmunity Testing & Root Cause Solutions

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

Autoimmunity is rarely a sudden event; it is a spectrum that develops over years. Conventional medicine often waits for the "scream"—when organ damage is visible. At Archangel Michael Health, we prefer to listen to the "whispers"—subtle shifts in blood markers that offer a window of opportunity. However, listening requires discernment. Not every ache or rash is autoimmune, and indiscriminate testing often leads to confusion rather than clarity.

On this page

1. When NOT to Order Tests

The "False Positive" Trap

It is critical to understand that autoimmune tests are not screening tests for the general healthy population. A positive result does not automatically mean you have a disease. Up to 30% of healthy individuals (especially women and the elderly) may have low-level positive antibodies without ever developing an autoimmune condition. Ordering an ANA for "general fatigue" or simple joint pain often leads to "rabbit holes" of anxiety and unnecessary medical workups.

Common Pitfalls: Confusing Simpler Conditions for Autoimmunity

Joint Pain
OSTEOARTHRITIS VS. RA
Osteoarthritis (OA)
Mechanism: "Wear and tear" mechanical degradation.
Key Symptom: Pain gets worse with use/activity. Morning stiffness lasts <30 minutes.
Location: Tips of fingers (DIP joints) and knees.
Testing: Do NOT order ANA. It is usually irrelevant.
Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
Mechanism: Inflammatory attack on synovium.
Key Symptom: Pain gets better with movement. Morning stiffness lasts >1 hour.
Location: Wrists and knuckles (MCP joints).
Testing: Order RF and Anti-CCP.
Facial Rashes
ROSACEA VS. LUPUS
Rosacea
Appearance: Redness on cheeks, often with pimples or pustules.
Triggers: Spicy food, alcohol, heat.
Key Sign: It affects the nasolabial folds (smile lines).
Testing: Do NOT order ANA.
Lupus Malar Rash
Appearance: "Butterfly" distribution across cheeks and nose.
Triggers: Sun exposure (photosensitive).
Key Sign: It spares the nasolabial folds (the area right next to the nose remains pale).
Testing: Order ANA.

2. The Barometers: ESR & CRP

Before identifying what is being attacked, we must establish if there is a systemic fire.

ESR (Sed Rate)
CHRONIC MARKER
What it is
Measures how fast red blood cells fall. Inflammation creates proteins (fibrinogen) that make cells sticky and heavy.
Interpretation
Optimal: <10 mm/hr (men), <15 mm/hr (women).
High: >40 mm/hr suggests significant inflammation.
Nuance: Obesity increases ESR because fat cells release inflammatory cytokines. A moderately high ESR in an overweight patient without joint pain is often metabolic, not autoimmune.
hs-CRP
ACUTE / SILENT MARKER
What it is
C-Reactive Protein is produced by the liver in direct response to immune signaling (IL-6). It rises within hours.
Interpretation
Optimal: <0.5 mg/L (Cool system).
Warning: 1.0–3.0 mg/L (Leaky gut, insulin resistance, or early reactivity).
Danger: >3.0 mg/L (High risk for cardiovascular events; active inflammation).

3. ANA & Genetic HLA Testing

We use antibody testing to detect active attacks, and genetic testing to understand your inherent risk profile.

Antinuclear Antibody (ANA)
ANTIBODY SCREEN
The Titer
1:40 / 1:80: Low Positive. Seen in 30% of healthy people, viral infections, or aging. rarely indicates active disease.
≥ 1:160: Clinical cutoff. Only ~5% of healthy people have this.
≥ 1:320: Strongly suggests underlying autoimmune process.
The Pattern
Homogeneous: Classic for Lupus (SLE).
Centromere: Specific for Limited Scleroderma (CREST).
Speckled: Non-specific; can be Sjogren's, Lupus, or general inflammation.
HLA Genetic Testing
GENETIC RISK
What it is
Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA) markers are genetic "barcodes" on your cells that help your immune system distinguish self from non-self. Certain codes are prone to confusion.
HLA-B27
Condition: Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS) and Reactive Arthritis.
When to order: For inflammatory back pain (pain at night, better with exercise) or uveitis (eye inflammation).
Meaning: It is a strong predisposing factor. Being positive does not guarantee disease, but increases probability significantly in the presence of symptoms.
HLA-DRB1
Condition: Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA).
Meaning: Known as the "Shared Epitope." Carriers are at much higher risk for RA, especially if they smoke. The gene interacts with smoking to trigger the formation of Anti-CCP antibodies.

4. Specific Disease Antibodies

Lupus (SLE)
DIAGNOSTIC
Anti-dsDNA
The Tracker. 97% specific for Lupus. Unlike other markers, it fluctuates with disease activity. Rising levels often predict a kidney flare (Nephritis).
Anti-Sm (Smith)
The Confirm-er. The most specific antibody for Lupus. If positive, it is virtually diagnostic.
Complement (C3/C4)
Low levels: Suggest active "consumption" by immune complexes (Lupus flare).
High levels: Suggest insulin resistance (metabolic syndrome).
Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
JOINT HEALTH
Rheumatoid Factor
The Old Guard. Non-specific; can be elevated in Hep C, Sjogren's, or healthy aging. Sensitivity is only ~70%.
Anti-CCP
The Sniper. 96-98% specific for RA. Can appear 10-15 years before joint pain begins. Strongly linked to smoking and gum disease.

5. Root Causes & Triggers

In the "Pre-Clinical" phase, identifying these triggers offers the best chance to turn off the process.

  • Intestinal Permeability ("Leaky Gut"): Gluten and dysbiosis trigger Zonulin release, opening gut barriers. This allows toxins into the blood, triggering "molecular mimicry" where the immune system attacks self-tissue.
  • Infections (EBV): The Epstein-Barr Virus (Mono) mimics human nuclear proteins. Chronic reactivation is a leading driver of ANA positivity.
  • Oral Health & Lungs: Gum disease bacteria (P. gingivalis) and smoking cause "citrullination" of proteins, driving Rheumatoid Arthritis.

6. Medications & Integrative Plan

We use a "treat-to-target" approach. While lifestyle is foundational, medications are essential when organs are threatened.

First-Line Medications
STANDARD OF CARE
Lupus (SLE)
Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil): The anchor drug. It prevents organ damage and flares. Generally well-tolerated but requires annual eye exams.
Mechanism: Modulates the immune system (via TLR inhibition) rather than suppressing it broadly.
Rheumatoid Arthritis
Methotrexate: The gold standard "Disease Modifying" drug (DMARD). Often started early to prevent joint erosion.
Biologics: Drugs like Abatacept or TNF-inhibitors are used if Methotrexate is insufficient.
Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)
Corticosteroids (Prednisone): PMR causes severe morning stiffness in shoulders/hips. It responds miraculously to low-dose prednisone (10-20mg). If symptoms don't resolve quickly, the diagnosis is likely wrong.
Ankylosing Spondylitis
NSAIDs: High-dose NSAIDs (like Naproxen/Indomethacin) are the first line.
TNF-Inhibitors: (e.g., Humira/Enbrel). Highly effective for spinal inflammation where Methotrexate often fails.
Integrative Strategy
LIFESTYLE RX
Nutrition
Mediterranean Diet: High in Omega-3s and polyphenols. Proven to reduce RA pain.
Autoimmune Protocol (AIP): For active flares. Eliminates grains, dairy, nightshades to "reset" the system.
Supplements
Vitamin D: Target 40-60 ng/mL. Deficiency correlates with Lupus/MS.
Omega-3s: 2-4g daily. Reduces inflammatory cytokines.
Curcumin: Potent anti-inflammatory for joint pain.
