Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) Score: Interpretation & Optimal Management

Patient Guide: Coronary Calcium Score (CAC), Atherosclerosis, and Practical Prevention

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

A Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) score (also called a “CT calcium scan”) is a low-radiation CT test that measures calcified plaque in the coronary arteries. Calcium itself is not the “enemy”; it is typically a marker of past arterial injury and the body’s attempt to stabilize plaque. What matters clinically is what a positive CAC score confirms: coronary atherosclerosis is present. That finding is actionable because atherosclerosis is a systemic, decades-long disease that can often be slowed dramatically—and sometimes partially reversed—by targeting the underlying drivers (lipids, blood pressure, insulin resistance, inflammation, smoking, sleep, and lifestyle).

Important: CAC is a risk marker, not a stress test. The next steps are driven by your symptoms, overall risk profile, and the specific CAC level—not the number alone.

Overview and key points

How to use this guide: (1) Identify your CAC category and percentile, (2) decide whether additional testing is warranted (usually symptom-driven), and (3) treat the underlying atherosclerosis with an intensity that matches your risk—often with a “lifestyle-first + appropriate medications” approach.

What CAC measures (and what it does not)

  • CAC measures calcified plaque (stable “scar-like” plaque) and correlates with total plaque burden.
  • CAC does not directly measure soft plaque (lipid-rich, inflamed plaque that can rupture). You can have meaningful soft plaque even with a CAC of 0—especially at younger ages or with high-risk conditions.
  • A high CAC does not automatically mean a critical blockage. It means you have atherosclerosis. Whether you have flow-limiting stenosis is determined by symptoms and/or further testing.
  • Most heart attacks come from plaque rupture and clot, not from slowly “clogging” to 100%. Treatment focuses on stabilizing plaque biology, not just chasing anatomy.

How the score is calculated

CAC is typically reported as an Agatston score, which weights both the area of calcium and its density. The scanner identifies calcium above a threshold density and sums calcification across the major coronary arteries (left main, LAD, LCX, RCA). The score is also often reported as an age/sex percentile, which can matter: a “low” absolute score may still be high relative to peers in younger individuals.

Interpretation by CAC category (high-yield)

  • CAC = 0: Very low short-term risk (“warranty period”), but not lifetime immunity. Focus on prevention. Statins are often deferred unless other high-risk features exist.
  • CAC 1–99: Early plaque. Risk is above zero; prevention intensity increases. Many patients benefit from at least moderate-intensity lipid-lowering and aggressive lifestyle optimization, especially if older, above the 75th percentile, or with additional risk enhancers.
  • CAC 100–399: Moderate plaque burden. Often treated with a “secondary prevention mindset” (aggressive LDL/ApoB lowering, blood pressure optimization, and consideration of aspirin if bleeding risk is low).
  • CAC ≥400: Extensive plaque. Risk is high enough that most clinicians treat as very high-risk and often involve cardiology for aggressive prevention and decisions about additional testing.
  • CAC ≥1000: A distinct “very high-risk” group. Treatment intensity typically matches chronic coronary disease/secondary prevention targets.

Why your score may rise even when you are improving (“statin paradox”)

CAC often increases over time—even with optimal therapy. This can reflect plaque stabilization: soft plaque becomes denser, more calcified, and less rupture-prone. For that reason, repeat CAC scans are not done frequently, and we do not “chase the number to zero.” We chase risk-factor control and plaque stability.

Symptoms and red flags

Many people with elevated CAC have no symptoms. When symptoms occur, they can reflect reduced blood flow (ischemia), plaque instability, rhythm disturbances, or other cardiac/pulmonary causes. The key is to treat symptoms as symptoms—do not rely on a CAC score (even a “good” one) to explain away concerning changes.

Common (but non-specific) symptoms that deserve evaluation

  • Exertional chest pressure, tightness, heaviness, or burning (sometimes felt in the left arm, jaw, back, or upper abdomen).
  • Shortness of breath with exertion that is new or worsening.
  • Reduced exercise capacity (you “run out of gas” sooner than expected).
  • Unexplained fatigue (particularly if exertional and new).
  • Palpitations, especially if accompanied by dizziness or breathlessness.
Red flags (same-day / emergency evaluation): Chest pain or pressure at rest or with minimal exertion; crescendo (worsening) chest discomfort; chest symptoms with sweating, nausea, shortness of breath, or radiation to arm/jaw/back; new shortness of breath at rest; fainting or near-fainting (especially with exertion); palpitations with lightheadedness.

Diagnosis and what to test

CAC is best viewed as an objective “receipt” that atherosclerosis is present. The clinical task is to (1) determine whether there is evidence of ischemia or unstable symptoms that warrants additional testing, and (2) identify the drivers of plaque so we can treat the disease biology.

Step 1: Review the CAC report carefully

  • Total Agatston score and age/sex percentile (high percentile suggests accelerated disease).
  • Distribution by artery (left main involvement can carry higher concern; diffuse multi-vessel calcium implies broader plaque burden).
  • Incidental findings sometimes appear (lung nodules, aortic valve calcification). These are addressed separately.

Step 2: Baseline cardiometabolic testing (treat the biology)

A CAC score is a prompt to measure and improve the most actionable drivers of plaque: apoB-containing lipoproteins, inflammation, blood pressure load, glucose/insulin signaling, and lifestyle foundations.

  • Lipid / particle risk: fasting lipid panel, non-HDL cholesterol, ApoB, and Lp(a) (once in adulthood, especially with family history).
  • Glycemic / metabolic risk: fasting glucose, HbA1c; consider fasting insulin and/or markers of insulin resistance when relevant.
  • Inflammation: hs-CRP can help quantify residual inflammatory risk (interpret in context; infections and injuries can raise it).
  • Kidney / electrolytes: CMP (creatinine/eGFR, liver enzymes, electrolytes) to guide medication selection.
  • Blood pressure quality: home BP log (7 days, seated, standardized technique) is often more useful than one clinic reading.
  • Other risk enhancers (case-by-case): thyroid status, sleep apnea evaluation, urine albumin/creatinine, and secondary causes of dyslipidemia (as clinically indicated).

Step 3: When do we add cardiac tests beyond CAC?

Additional testing is usually driven by symptoms, a plan to begin vigorous exercise in higher-risk patients, unusual findings on the CAC report, or uncertainty about whether there is flow-limiting disease.

Exercise treadmill ECG stress test
Functional test
What it does
Monitors ECG and blood pressure during exercise to look for ischemic changes.
Best for
Patients able to exercise with interpretable ECG and a lower-to-intermediate pretest probability.
Limitations
Less sensitive than imaging stress tests; false positives can occur; does not show plaque directly.
When we consider it
New exertional symptoms or risk stratification before starting high-intensity exercise in select higher-risk patients.
Stress echocardiogram
Functional + imaging
What it does
Ultrasound images of heart wall motion at rest and during stress (exercise or medication). Reduced wall motion under stress suggests ischemia.
Best for
Symptomatic patients where we want physiologic information without radiation; useful when ECG is difficult to interpret.
Limitations
Image quality can be limited in some body types; operator-dependent.
When we consider it
Exertional symptoms, intermediate suspicion for ischemia, or exercise clearance in selected higher-risk patients.
Nuclear stress test (myocardial perfusion imaging)
Functional + imaging
What it does
Uses a tracer to map blood flow to heart muscle at rest and stress; identifies reversible ischemia (a pattern that can suggest a flow-limiting blockage).
Best for
Patients who cannot exercise well or when higher sensitivity is needed; useful in certain complex cases.
Limitations
Radiation exposure; false positives/negatives can occur; does not directly show plaque morphology.
When we consider it
Symptoms concerning for ischemia, equivocal initial testing, or when a clearer physiologic answer is needed.
Coronary CT angiography (CCTA)
Anatomic test
What it does
Uses IV contrast to directly visualize coronary anatomy, including non-calcified plaque and degree of stenosis. Helpful for “plaqueology” (plaque type), not just “lumenology.”
Best for
Intermediate cases where symptoms or risk profile warrant anatomic clarification; excellent for ruling out significant blockages.
Limitations
Uses contrast and radiation; heavy calcification (often CAC >1000) can limit accuracy due to blooming artifact.
When we consider it
Concerning symptoms with uncertain diagnosis, equivocal stress test results, or when anatomy will meaningfully change management.
Resting echocardiogram
Structure + function
What it does
Assesses heart pumping function (ejection fraction), chamber size, valves, and wall thickness.
What it does not do
It does not directly diagnose coronary blockages.
When we consider it
Shortness of breath, abnormal exam, murmurs, heart failure symptoms, or as part of a broader cardiac workup.
Cardiac catheterization (invasive coronary angiography)
Definitive anatomy
What it does
An invasive test that threads a catheter from wrist or groin to the coronary arteries, injects dye, and records live X‑ray movies. It provides definitive anatomy and allows treatment (stent) if appropriate.
When we consider it
High-risk symptoms, a strongly positive stress test, high clinical suspicion, or when intervention is being considered.
Key nuance
In stable coronary disease, modern evidence shows that optimized medical therapy and lifestyle can be as effective as stents for preventing heart attacks and death; stents are most valuable for acute heart attack or for persistent, limiting angina despite medical therapy.

Root causes and contributing factors

Atherosclerosis is not a “cholesterol-only” problem. It is a chronic injury-and-repair process in the artery wall. Cholesterol-containing particles and immune cells participate in the repair response; persistent injury keeps the process active, promoting growth of soft plaque and eventual calcification.

High-yield drivers (what we can often change)

  • ApoB particle burden: LDL and other ApoB-containing particles drive plaque growth. “Normal” LDL may be too high for your personal plaque biology when CAC is elevated.
  • Blood pressure load: Chronic shear stress damages the endothelial lining and accelerates plaque.
  • Insulin resistance / diabetes: High glucose and metabolic dysfunction increase oxidative stress and glycation, worsening vascular injury.
  • Tobacco exposure: A direct arterial toxin; “light” smoking and vaping still carry meaningful risk.
  • Inflammation: Periodontal disease, chronic stress, poor sleep, untreated sleep apnea, autoimmune disease, and visceral adiposity can amplify vascular inflammation.
  • Sedentary physiology: Low cardiorespiratory fitness worsens endothelial function and metabolic health.
  • Diet quality: Ultra-processed foods, refined sugars, and “industrial” fats can worsen lipids, insulin resistance, and inflammation; nutrient-dense whole-food patterns (Mediterranean or whole-food plant-forward) are consistently associated with lower event rates.

Risk enhancers that often change treatment intensity

  • Strong family history of premature coronary disease
  • Elevated Lp(a)
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Inflammatory disorders (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis)
  • Obstructive sleep apnea
  • Persistent hypertriglyceridemia
  • Premature menopause or a history of pregnancy-related vascular complications

Step-by-step improvement plan

The goal is not simply to “have a lower CAC number.” The goal is to reduce near-term risk by stabilizing plaque and reducing the drivers that create new plaque. Your CAC score helps set the intensity of your prevention plan.

Step 0: Safety screen

  • If you have red-flag symptoms, seek urgent evaluation regardless of CAC.
  • If you have known coronary disease (prior heart attack, stent, bypass), CAC adds little—management follows chronic coronary disease standards.

Step 1: Map your risk and choose targets

In Archangel Michael Health’s framework, higher CAC pushes us toward lower LDL/ApoB targets and more aggressive treatment of blood pressure, glucose, weight, and inflammation. Targets are individualized, but typical prevention goals for higher CAC are more stringent than standard “primary prevention.”

  • Blood pressure: typically <130/80 mmHg; in very high-risk patients, we may aim closer to a systolic around 120 if tolerated.
  • LDL-C and ApoB: mild CAC often targets LDL <100; moderate CAC often targets LDL <70; severe CAC often targets LDL <55 with ApoB targets that match that intensity.
  • Triglycerides: ideally <150 mg/dL; persistently higher values usually signal insulin resistance, alcohol effect, or genetic predisposition.
  • Glucose control: HbA1c and fasting glucose in an individualized healthy range; focus on insulin sensitivity rather than “normal labs with abnormal physiology.”
  • Inflammation: address root causes (sleep, weight, periodontal health, exercise, stress); hs-CRP is one optional marker for tracking in context.

Step 3: Build your personal 90-day action plan (printable checklist)

Atherosclerosis responds best to consistent, repeatable behaviors. Choose a few high-leverage changes and execute them relentlessly.

  • Nutrition (choose 3): ☐ Mediterranean pattern as default ☐ 30+ grams/day fiber (legumes, oats, psyllium) ☐ 2 servings/week fatty fish (or equivalent omega‑3 plan) ☐ replace refined carbs with minimally processed carbs ☐ eliminate sugar-sweetened beverages ☐ reduce ultra-processed snacks ☐ increase protein quality (fish, legumes, poultry) ☐ focus on olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado as primary fats
  • Exercise: ☐ 150 minutes/week moderate aerobic OR 75 minutes vigorous (scaled to your level) ☐ 2 strength sessions/week ☐ daily walking target ☐ consider supervised cardiac rehab if high risk or deconditioned
  • Sleep: ☐ consistent sleep window ☐ 7–9 hours/night ☐ screen for sleep apnea if loud snoring, witnessed apneas, or persistent fatigue
  • Stress physiology: ☐ 10 minutes/day breathwork or mindfulness ☐ structured recovery days ☐ social connection/support
  • Risk toxins: ☐ no tobacco/nicotine ☐ alcohol within low-risk limits (or avoid if triglycerides, blood pressure, or sleep suffer) ☐ address periodontal health
  • Tracking: ☐ home BP log weekly ☐ weight/waist monthly ☐ lipid/apoB recheck 8–12 weeks after changes ☐ symptom and exercise tolerance log

Conventional treatments

Medication decisions should match CAC-informed risk and your individual drivers (lipids, BP, glucose, inflammation). Most of the benefit comes from reducing ApoB particles, stabilizing plaque biology, and lowering thrombosis risk when appropriate.

Statins (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin)
First-line lipid lowering
What they are
Medications that reduce LDL-C and ApoB particle burden and reduce vascular inflammation.
How they work
Decrease cholesterol synthesis in the liver and increase LDL receptor activity, lowering circulating ApoB particles.
Typical use
Moderate or high intensity depending on CAC level and risk. Recheck lipids/ApoB 6–12 weeks after starting or dose changes.
Common side effects
Muscle aches, mild liver enzyme changes; rare serious muscle injury. Many symptoms are manageable by dose changes, alternate-day dosing, or switching agents.
Approximate cost
Often low-cost generics; cash prices vary by pharmacy and dose.
Ezetimibe
Add-on lipid lowering
What it is
A medication that lowers LDL-C by reducing cholesterol absorption in the intestine.
When we use it
When LDL/ApoB targets are not met with statin alone, or when statins are not tolerated at effective doses.
Side effects
Usually well tolerated; occasional GI symptoms or mild lab changes.
Approximate cost
Often inexpensive as a generic.
PCSK9 inhibitors (e.g., evolocumab, alirocumab) and inclisiran
Potent LDL/ApoB lowering
What they are
Injectable therapies that dramatically lower LDL-C and ApoB particles. Inclisiran is a twice-yearly siRNA approach after initial dosing.
When we use them
Very high-risk patients (often CAC ≥400–1000+), those with familial hypercholesterolemia, or when LDL targets are not achieved with statin + ezetimibe.
Side effects
Injection site reactions; otherwise generally well tolerated.
Approximate cost
Typically high without insurance coverage; patient assistance programs may apply.
Bempedoic acid
Non-statin LDL lowering
What it is
An oral LDL-lowering agent that can be useful when statins are not tolerated or insufficient.
Side effects
Can raise uric acid (gout risk) and rarely affect tendons; individualized monitoring is important.
Approximate cost
Typically moderate-to-high depending on coverage.
Aspirin (low-dose, typically 81 mg)
Antiplatelet
Why it matters
Most heart attacks are triggered by a clot forming over a plaque rupture. Aspirin reduces platelet stickiness.
When we use it
Not for everyone. It is often considered when CAC is ≥100 (especially ≥300) and bleeding risk is low, and more strongly considered at higher CAC levels—after individualized counseling.
Key risks
GI bleeding and hemorrhagic stroke risk. Avoid if bleeding risk is elevated or on certain anticoagulants unless explicitly advised.
Approximate cost
Low.
Blood pressure medications (ACE/ARB, thiazide, calcium-channel blocker, etc.)
Vascular protection
Why they matter
Lowering BP reduces endothelial shear stress and lowers stroke/heart attack risk even independent of cholesterol.
How we choose
Based on BP pattern, kidney function, potassium, diabetes, and side-effect profile; home BP logs guide decisions.
Approximate cost
Often low-cost generics.
Cardiometabolic medications for diabetes and weight (GLP‑1 receptor agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors)
Risk reduction beyond glucose
Why they matter
These agents can lower cardiovascular events in high-risk patients and improve multiple plaque drivers (weight, BP, glycemia).
Typical use
Considered in patients with diabetes, obesity, or high cardiometabolic risk where lifestyle alone is insufficient.
Approximate cost
Often high without coverage; varies widely.
Icosapent ethyl (EPA)
Triglyceride-related risk
Who it helps
Patients with elevated triglycerides despite optimized LDL treatment, particularly in higher-risk categories.
Mechanism (simplified)
Improves triglyceride metabolism and may reduce inflammation and plaque vulnerability.
Side effects
GI upset in some; small increased risk of atrial fibrillation or bleeding in certain populations.
Approximate cost
Variable; generic availability and coverage matter.
Low-dose colchicine (selected high-risk patients)
Anti-inflammatory (emerging)
Why it is considered
Residual inflammatory risk can persist even with excellent LDL control. Colchicine may reduce vascular inflammation and events in selected chronic coronary disease patients.
Cautions
Drug interactions (especially with certain antibiotics and statins), kidney/liver considerations, and GI side effects require clinician oversight.
Approximate cost
Often modest as a generic, depending on dosing and pharmacy.

Integrative and lifestyle-first strategies

Integrative prevention is not “anti-medication.” It is stacking interventions: nutrition, movement, sleep, stress physiology, and evidence-informed supplements—layered on top of appropriate conventional therapy. Lifestyle is the foundation; supplements are adjuncts.

Nutrition: the highest ROI intervention

  • Mediterranean pattern: vegetables, legumes, fruits, nuts, whole grains; olive oil as primary fat; fish regularly; minimal ultra-processed foods.
  • Whole-food plant-forward / Ornish-style: particularly effective for some patients with high LDL/ApoB and inflammatory patterns; emphasizes very low saturated fat and high fiber.
  • Insulin resistance strategy: for high triglycerides, fatty liver, or metabolic syndrome, focus on reducing refined carbs and liquid calories, increasing protein quality, and increasing activity after meals.
  • Practical “swap list”: beans/lentils instead of refined grains; fruit or yogurt instead of pastries; nuts instead of chips; olive oil and herbs instead of sugary sauces.

Exercise: plaque biology and endothelial function

  • Aerobic base: aim for consistent zone-2 style work (brisk walking, cycling) as the default.
  • Strength training: twice weekly supports insulin sensitivity and healthy body composition.
  • HIIT (optional): can be time-efficient; best introduced after an aerobic base is established. In higher CAC categories, progress gradually and consider stress testing or supervised rehab before vigorous programs.

Sleep and stress physiology

  • Sleep: poor sleep raises BP, worsens glucose control, and increases appetite hormones. Treat sleep apnea when present—this is often a high-leverage “hidden driver.”
  • Stress: chronic sympathetic activation increases inflammation and impairs behavior change. A small daily stress practice (breathwork, prayer/meditation, nature walks) can be a meaningful biologic intervention.

Evidence-informed supplement options (adjuncts)

Supplements can be useful when chosen with a clear goal, appropriate dosing, and attention to interactions. Quality matters. If you take anticoagulants, have kidney/liver disease, are pregnant, or are on complex medications, review supplements with your clinician.

What we avoid in an evidence-informed practice

  • Replacing proven therapies (like statins when indicated) with supplements alone.
  • Unmonitored high-dose fat-soluble vitamins without a clear indication.
  • Frequent repeat CAC scanning as the primary feedback mechanism.

Cost considerations

Costs vary widely by region, insurance, and facility. The goal is to spend money where it buys meaningful risk reduction: high-value labs, high-yield lifestyle interventions, and medications with strong outcomes data when indicated.

  • CAC scan: often a few hundred dollars cash-pay (varies by market).
  • Core labs: lipid panel and metabolic panel are usually low-cost; ApoB and Lp(a) may be additional.
  • Stress testing: treadmill ECG tends to be less expensive than imaging stress tests; nuclear testing is often higher cost.
  • CCTA: moderate-to-high cost; includes contrast and advanced imaging interpretation.
  • Cardiac catheterization: high cost and invasive; reserved for situations where it is likely to change care.
  • Medications: many foundational therapies are inexpensive generics; newer lipid and metabolic agents may be expensive without coverage.
  • Supplements: costs add up quickly; we prioritize a small number of high-leverage adjuncts (if any) over large “stacks.”

FAQ

Should I repeat my CAC scan to see if I’m improving?

Usually not soon. CAC can rise even with good therapy due to plaque stabilization. We typically track improvement using biomarkers (LDL/ApoB, BP, A1c/insulin resistance markers), fitness, symptoms, and weight/waist—then consider repeat CAC only if it will meaningfully change decisions.

If I have a high score, do I automatically need a stent?

No. CAC confirms atherosclerosis but does not prove a flow-limiting blockage. Stents are most beneficial for acute heart attacks or persistent limiting angina despite optimal medical therapy. Many stable patients do best with aggressive lifestyle + medications.

Can I have a heart attack with a CAC score of 0?

Yes, although risk is low. CAC of 0 does not rule out non-calcified plaque, spasm, clotting disorders, or non-coronary causes. Symptoms always override CAC.

Does high calcium mean I should stop calcium supplements or avoid dietary calcium?

Not necessarily. Vascular calcium is part of plaque biology, not simply “too much dietary calcium.” Decisions about calcium supplements depend on bone health, diet, and kidney stone risk. We individualize this discussion and often focus more on vitamin D status, magnesium intake, and overall cardiometabolic control than on calcium avoidance.

What is the single best thing I can do?

For most patients: eliminate tobacco exposure, build a sustainable whole-food nutrition pattern, improve fitness, and lower ApoB particle burden to an appropriate target for your CAC level—often the most powerful combination.
