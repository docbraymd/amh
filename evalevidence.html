Evaluating Healthcare Evidence

A Clinical Guide for the Sophisticated Patient

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

At Archangel Michael Health, we operate on the principles of preventative medicine and treating problems early with common sense. However, in the 21st century, the greatest threat to your preventative health plan is often the "Infodemic"—a chaotic environment where peer-reviewed science competes for your attention against viral anecdotes and corporate marketing. To be an effective partner in your own healthcare, you must treat "Misinformation" as a condition to be diagnosed and managed.

On this page

1. Clinical Presentation: Symptoms of Misinformation

Just as a metabolic disease has physical symptoms, exposure to poor-quality evidence presents with specific cognitive and emotional signs. If you encounter information displaying these characteristics, proceed with caution:

  • The "Miracle" Narrative: Claims of "secret cures," "ancient remedies doctors hide," or supplements that cure disparate conditions (e.g., cancer, diabetes, and fatigue simultaneously).
  • Emotional Hyper-Arousal: Headlines designed to trigger immediate fear ("The silent killer in your pantry") or outrage. Misinformation spreads 70% faster than truth because it exploits high-arousal emotions.
  • The "N of 1" Fallacy: Reliance on individual testimonials ("It worked for my cousin") rather than data. This ignores the Placebo Effect and the natural course of disease (Regression to the Mean).
  • Absolute Certainty: Science deals in probabilities; marketing deals in guarantees. Terms like "100% effective" or "chemical-free" are diagnostic red flags.

2. Diagnosis: The Hierarchy of Evidence

To diagnose the validity of a health claim, we must determine where it sits on the "Hierarchy of Evidence." Relying on low-level evidence is the primary cause of poor health decisions.

Diagnostic Criteria

Level 1 (Gold Standard): Systematic Reviews & Meta-Analyses. These combine data from multiple studies to filter out noise. If a meta-analysis confirms a treatment, it is highly reliable.

Level 2: Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs). Participants are randomly assigned to treatment or placebo. This eliminates "Selection Bias." Without randomization, we cannot prove a treatment works.

Level 3: Observational Studies. Researchers observe correlations (e.g., "Wine drinkers live longer"). These are prone to "Confounders" (e.g., wine drinkers may be wealthier, and wealth extends life, not the wine).

Level 4: Anecdotes & Expert Opinion. The lowest form of evidence. A story is not data.

3. Root Causes: Systemic & Psychological Factors

Why is the information landscape so toxic? The pathology is two-fold:

The Commercial Determinants of Health

Financial conflicts of interest exist across the spectrum. The Pharmaceutical Industry has a history of "ghostwriting" studies and paying "patient advocacy groups" (Astroturfing). Conversely, the Supplement Industry operates with minimal oversight under the DSHEA Act; products reach the market without proving safety or efficacy, and FDA testing often reveals contamination.

The Cognitive Factors

Our brains are wired for survival, not statistics. The Availability Heuristic makes us fear rare events (like a plane crash) because the stories are vivid, while ignoring common risks. The Confirmation Bias leads us to click on headlines that validate what we already believe.

4. Consequences of Poor Evidence

Failure to critically evaluate sources leads to specific adverse outcomes:

  • Financial Toxicity: Wasting resources on "wellness" products that act merely as expensive placebos.
  • Medical Non-Adherence: Stopping life-saving medication due to internet rumors or "Nocebo" effects (believing a safe drug is harmful).
  • Direct Harm: Patients using alternative therapies in place of conventional care for cancer have been shown to have significantly higher mortality rates.

5. Treatment Plan: Evidence-Based "Prescriptions"

As your physician, I am prescribing the following "Intellectual Medications." These are tools you should use whenever you are considering a new supplement, lifestyle change, or medical intervention.

Rx: The CRAAP Test
DIAGNOSTIC PROTOCOL
What It Is
A rapid screening protocol to evaluate the credibility of any website or article.
How It Works
Forces a logical pause to override emotional reaction.
Currency: Is it up to date?
Relevance: Does it apply to humans?
Authority: Is the author a qualified medical expert?
Accuracy: Are there citations to peer-reviewed RCTs?
Purpose: Are they educating you or selling you a product?
Cost
$0.00 (Requires 2–5 minutes).
Typical Usage
Apply immediately upon reading any health headline that evokes fear or excitement.
Rx: PubMed / Cochrane
REFERENCE DATABASE
What It Is
PubMed: The NIH database of biomedical literature. Cochrane: The gold-standard repository for Systematic Reviews.
How It Works
Provides access to raw primary data, bypassing media spin. Searching for "Systematic Review" + [Topic] reveals the consensus of the scientific community.
Side Effects
Medical jargon can be dense. Tip: Read the "Abstract" and "Plain Language Summary" first.
Typical Usage
Use to verify specific claims (e.g., "Does Turmeric help arthritis?") before purchasing supplements.
Rx: Open Payments
TRANSPARENCY TOOL
What It Is
A federal tool (CMS.gov) tracking payments from drug/device companies to physicians.
How It Works
Reveals financial conflicts of interest. If an "expert" is aggressively promoting a specific branded drug, check if they are being paid by the manufacturer.
Side Effects
Increased skepticism of "Key Opinion Leaders" and paid hospital speakers.
Typical Usage
Consult before agreeing to high-cost branded interventions.
Rx: Statistical Literacy
COGNITIVE SUPPLEMENT
What It Is
Understanding Relative Risk vs. Absolute Risk.
How It Works
Headlines often use Relative Risk ("Drug X reduces risk by 50%") to sound dramatic. If baseline risk is 2 in 1000, a 50% reduction only lowers it to 1 in 1000 (Absolute Risk reduction of 0.1%).
Side Effects
Immunity to clickbait headlines claiming "massive spikes" in risk or benefit.
Typical Usage
Use whenever a news report claims a "major breakthrough" or a "significant increase" in danger.
