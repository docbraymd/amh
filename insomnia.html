Insomnia: Restoring Sleep Architecture

A Comprehensive Protocol for Reclaiming Restorative Rest

by Christopher L. Bray MD PhD

In the contemporary landscape of internal medicine, few physiological processes are as fundamental to human health—and yet as profoundly disrupted—as sleep. For the patients of Archangel Michael Health, understanding the mechanisms of sleep is a critical step toward reclaiming vitality, metabolic stability, and cognitive longevity. We view chronic insomnia not merely as a "deficiency of sleeping pills," but often as a dysregulation of the body’s biological "hardware" (circadian rhythms, neurochemistry) and "software" (habits, stress response). This guide provides an advanced, integrative roadmap to identify root causes and restore natural sleep architecture.

On this page

1. The Science of Sleep Drive

To treat insomnia effectively, we must understand the "Two-Process Model" of sleep regulation. Dysfunction in either process leads to the "tired but wired" state common in modern patients.

  • Process S (Sleep Pressure): This is metabolic. As your neurons fire throughout the day, they burn energy (ATP) and accumulate a byproduct called adenosine. Like a steam valve, adenosine pressure builds up over 16 hours of wakefulness, eventually triggering the "sleep gate" to open. Caffeine works by chemically blocking the sensors that detect this pressure.
  • Process C (Circadian Rhythm): This is your 24-hour internal clock, anchored by light and dark. Paradoxically, your circadian clock provides an "alerting signal" in the early evening to keep you awake against the building adenosine pressure. In healthy sleep, this alerting signal drops off rapidly at night, allowing melatonin to rise.
  • The Mismatch: Chronic insomnia often occurs when these two forces are misaligned—for example, if evening blue light keeps the circadian alerting signal active too late, or if caffeine or napping prevents adenosine pressure from reaching critical mass.

2. Symptoms and Red Flags

Common Presentations

  • Sleep-Onset Insomnia: Taking longer than 30 minutes to fall asleep. Often driven by anxiety, racing thoughts (glutamate/cortisol excess), or delayed circadian rhythm (night owls).
  • Sleep-Maintenance Insomnia: Waking frequently during the night or waking at 2–3 AM and unable to return to sleep. Often driven by blood sugar instability (nocturnal hypoglycemia), alcohol rebound, or sleep apnea.
  • Early Morning Awakening: Waking fully alert at 4 AM. Common in depression or advanced age (circadian phase advance).

Red Flags: When to Look Deeper

Before assuming you have primary insomnia, we must rule out physical blockers. If you have the following signs, standard sleep restriction therapy may be unsafe until these are addressed:

  • Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): Loud snoring, gasping/choking sounds, or waking up unrefreshed despite 7+ hours in bed. OSA fragments sleep architecture and requires a sleep study (polysomnography) or home sleep test.
  • Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS): An irresistible urge to move legs, worse at night or at rest. This is often linked to dopamine dysfunction and low brain iron. We screen for this by checking Ferritin levels; levels below 50-75 ng/mL can cause symptoms even if not technically "anemic".

3. Root Causes (Hardware vs. Software)

The "Tired but Wired" Phenotype

Many patients suffer from 24-hour hyperarousal. Their cortisol (stress hormone) fails to drop in the evening. This keeps the nervous system in a sympathetic "fight or flight" state, suppressing the deep, restorative Slow Wave Sleep (SWS).

Contributors to Insomnia
DIAGNOSTICS
Light Environment
The Hardware Problem: We are an "indoor species" living in biological twilight. Insufficient morning light fails to anchor the rhythm, while evening blue light (screens/LEDs) suppresses melatonin and shifts the clock later.
Metabolic Factors
Nocturnal Hypoglycemia: A drop in blood sugar at 2-3 AM can trigger an adrenaline surge to liberate stored glucose, waking you up with a racing heart. This is common in patients who eat high-carb/sugar dinners or drink alcohol close to bed.
Genetic Factors
Caffeine Metabolism (CYP1A2): "Slow metabolizers" may still have 25-50% of their morning caffeine active in their brain at bedtime, blocking deep sleep signals even if they can "fall asleep" easily.
Thermoregulation
For sleep onset, core body temperature must drop by ~2-3°F. Inability to "dump heat" (often due to cold hands/feet causing vasoconstriction) can prevent sleep onset.

4. The Archangel Michael Protocol

We utilize a tiered approach, starting with foundational "hardware" updates (light/temperature) and "software" updates (CBT-I behaviors).

What "Better" Looks Like

Success is defined as high Sleep Efficiency: spending at least 85-90% of your time in bed actually sleeping, rather than tossing and turning. The goal is consolidated, continuous rest, not just "more hours."

  • Step 1: Morning Anchors (The Reset). Wake at the same time every single day (even weekends). View 10-20 minutes of outdoor sunlight within 30 minutes of waking. This sets the timer for melatonin release 14 hours later and anchors your cortisol awakening response.
  • Step 2: Caffeine Fade. Stop all caffeine by 12:00 PM (noon). If you are a slow metabolizer or have severe maintenance insomnia, a 4-week complete washout may be necessary to reset adenosine receptors.
  • Step 3: Thermal Trigger. Take a warm bath or shower 90 minutes before bed. This dilates blood vessels, allowing heat to escape your core when you step into a cool room (65°F), triggering the biological sleep signal.
  • Step 4: Stimulus Control (The 20-Minute Rule). If you cannot sleep after ~20 minutes, get out of bed. Go to a dim room and read a boring book. Return only when sleepy. This breaks the brain's conditioned association that "Bed = Anxiety".
  • Step 5: Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR). If you wake and cannot return to sleep, perform an NSDR protocol, a body scan, or 4-7-8 breathing exercises. This shifts the nervous system into rest mode even without unconsciousness, reducing sleep anxiety.

5. Integrative Therapeutics

While prescription sedatives often force unconsciousness (sometimes suppressing deep sleep), these integrative options aim to provide the raw materials for natural relaxation pathways (GABA, Alpha waves, Thermoregulation).

Magnesium Glycinate
RELAXATION
Rationale
Magnesium is a cofactor for GABA (the "braking" neurotransmitter). Glycine is an inhibitory amino acid that also lowers core body temperature.
Typical Use
200–400 mg taken 45–60 minutes before bed.
Cost
~$15–25 / month. Low cost.
Notes
Avoid Magnesium Oxide (poorly absorbed). Magnesium Citrate can cause loose stools. Magnesium L-Threonate is an alternative for cognitive "racing mind" but is more expensive.
L-Theanine
CALM FOCUS
Rationale
Amino acid found in green tea. Increases Alpha brain waves (associated with "wakeful relaxation" and meditation) rather than forcing sedation.
Typical Use
200–400 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Can be combined with Magnesium.
Cost
~$15–20 / month.
Notes
Non-habit forming and generally safe. Particularly useful for "racing thoughts" or anxiety-driven insomnia.
Low-Dose Melatonin
CIRCADIAN
Rationale
A signal to the brain that it is night, not a sedative. Best for "night owls" (delayed phase), shift work, or age-related melatonin decline.
Typical Use
Micro-dose: 0.3 mg to 1 mg taken 60–90 minutes before bed. "Less is more."
Cost
Very low.
Notes
High doses (5-10mg) found in stores can cause receptor down-regulation, grogginess, and vivid dreams. Use pharmaceutical grade if possible.
Glycine
THERMAL
Rationale
Simple amino acid that promotes peripheral vasodilation (lowering core temperature) and acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter.
Typical Use
3 grams (powder or capsule) dissolved in water/tea before bed.
Cost
~$20 / month.
Notes
Very safe profile. Can improve subjective sleep quality and reduce next-day fatigue.

6. Prescription Options

Medications should generally be used for short-term bridging or "rescue" while behavioral changes take root. We prioritize newer agents that preserve sleep architecture over older sedatives that may induce "pseudo-sleep."

Dual Orexin Receptor Antagonists (DORAs)
TARGETED
Examples
Suvorexant (Belsomra), Lemborexant (Dayvigo), Daridorexant (Quviviq).
How it works
Blocks Orexin, the brain's "wakefulness" chemical. Instead of sedating the whole brain (like Valium), it specifically turns off the "wake drive." Preserves REM/Deep sleep better than older drugs.
Typical Use
Taken nightly or as needed. Considered safe for longer-term use than Z-drugs.
Cost
High ($$$). Often requires prior authorization or coupons.
Side Effects
Next-day drowsiness (rare), sleep paralysis (rare).
Z-Drugs (Sedative Hypnotics)
SHORT-TERM
Examples
Zolpidem (Ambien), Eszopiclone (Lunesta), Zaleplon (Sonata).
How it works
Binds to GABA receptors to induce widespread sedation. "Forces" sleep.
Typical Use
Short-term (2-4 weeks) or intermittent "rescue" use.
Cost
Generic forms are very affordable ($).
Side Effects
Complex sleep behaviors (sleep eating/walking), dependence, tolerance, suppression of deep sleep waves.
