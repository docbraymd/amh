Patient Guide: Lifestyle, Integrative & Conventional Care

Overview: The Migraine Brain

Current science views migraine as a state of sensory processing sensitivity. The migraine brain does not filter stimuli—light, sound, smell—as effectively as a non-migraine brain. This "hyperexcitability" means your threshold for a neurological event is lower. When that threshold is crossed, the trigeminovascular system activates, releasing inflammatory chemicals like CGRP (Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide) that cause pain signals to bombard the brainstem.

  • Key point: Migraine is a genetic "hardware" issue, often involving ion channel variances that affect how nerves fire.
  • Key point: Treatment is not just about killing pain; it is about raising your threshold so attacks occur less often.
  • Key point: "Treat Early" is the golden rule. Treating during the mild headache phase is far more effective than waiting for severe pain.

Phases, Symptoms & Red Flags

The Four Phases

A migraine attack is a dynamic event that can span days. Recognizing the early phases allows you to intervene before the pain becomes disabling.

  • 1. Prodrome (The Warning): Occurs 24–48 hours before pain. Symptoms include frequent yawning, neck stiffness, food cravings (sweets/carbs), mood changes, and increased urination.
  • 2. Aura: Occurs in 25–30% of patients, typically lasting 5–60 minutes. Visual auras (zigzag lines, blind spots) are most common, but sensory aura (tingling hand/face) or speech difficulty can occur.
  • 3. Headache (The Attack): Lasts 4–72 hours. Pain is often one-sided and throbbing, worsened by movement. Nausea and sensitivity to light/sound are hallmarks.
  • 4. Postdrome (The Hangover): Lasts 24–48 hours after pain stops. Characterized by "brain fog," fatigue, and scalp tenderness.

Red Flags (SNOOP10 Criteria)

While migraines are not dangerous, certain headaches require urgent evaluation to rule out strokes, aneurysms, or infections. Seek immediate care for:

  • Thunderclap Headache: Sudden, severe pain reaching maximum intensity in seconds ("worst of life").
  • Systemic symptoms: Headache accompanied by fever, stiff neck, or weight loss.
  • Neurological deficits: Weakness, confusion, seizure, or vision loss that does not resolve within 60 minutes.
  • Older Onset: New headache pattern starting after age 50.
  • Positional: Pain triggers specifically when standing up or lying down.

Root Causes & Threshold Theory

We use the Threshold Theory to explain attacks. Imagine a bucket. Your genetics determine the size of the bucket. Triggers are the water filling it. When the bucket overflows, you get a migraine. You cannot change your genetics, but you can stop the bucket from overflowing by managing these drivers.

Common Triggers (The "Water")
MODIFIABLE
Stress Physiology
The "Let-down" headache often occurs on weekends when cortisol drops rapidly after a stressful week. Consistency in stress management is key.
Sleep
The migraine brain craves routine. Too little sleep, too much sleep, or irregular wake times (even on weekends) are potent triggers.
Medications
Certain medications can lower the threshold or trigger attacks. Oral Contraceptives: Estrogen-containing birth control can trigger attacks and increase stroke risk in patients with aura. Vasodilators: Drugs like nitroglycerin or PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra/Cialis) are known triggers.
Hormones
Estrogen withdrawal just before menstruation triggers "menstrual migraine," which is often more severe and resistant to treatment.

Dietary Triggers: MSG, Cheese & Wine

While some food "triggers" (like chocolate) are actually prodrome cravings, certain chemical compounds in food are well-documented triggers for susceptible patients. Identifying and limiting these can significantly raise your migraine threshold.

Key Chemical Triggers
DIET
Tyramine (Aged Foods)
What it is: A compound found in foods that have been aged, fermented, or stored for long periods.
Common Sources: Aged cheeses (Blue, Cheddar, Swiss, Parmesan), cured or smoked meats (salami, pepperoni), fermented soy (miso, tofu, soy sauce), and tap beer.
Mechanism: Tyramine can cause the release of norepinephrine, leading to vasoconstriction followed by rebound dilation and pain.
MSG (Monosodium Glutamate)
What it is: A flavor enhancer used in processed foods and some restaurant cooking.
Common Sources: Canned soups, processed snacks (chips, flavored crackers), fast food, seasoning blends, and soy sauce.
Mechanism: Glutamate is an excitatory neurotransmitter. In a hyperexcitable migraine brain, excess glutamate can trigger the electrical cascade of an attack.
Alcohol & Nitrates
Alcohol: Red wine is the most frequent culprit due to tannins, sulfites, and phenolic flavonoids. Clear spirits (vodka, gin) are often better tolerated. Alcohol also causes dehydration.
Nitrates: Found in bacon, hot dogs, and deli meats. They are direct vasodilators that can immediately trigger vascular headaches.
Aspartame
What it is: An artificial sweetener found in "diet" sodas and sugar-free snacks.
Action: A reported trigger for many patients; consider switching to natural sweeteners or water if you consume high amounts of diet soda.

Clinical Strategy

Do not restrict all these foods at once forever. We recommend a 4-6 week elimination period of high-risk items (Tyramine, MSG, Nitrates). Reintroduce them one by one to see which specific chemical affects your brain. Hydration and stable blood sugar (never skipping meals) are just as important as avoiding specific foods.

The "SEEDS" Improvement Plan

What "Better" Looks Like

We define success as a 50% reduction in headache days, reduced attack severity, and improved response to acute medication. Complete cure is rarely the goal; management and resilience are.

Implementing the SEEDS protocol is the foundation of preventative care.

  • S - Sleep: Wake up and go to bed at the same time every day. Screen for sleep apnea if you snore.
  • E - Exercise: Moderate aerobic exercise (30-50 mins, 3x/week) acts like a "natural beta-blocker" by burning off excess adrenaline.
  • E - Eat/Hydrate: Eat protein-rich meals every 3-4 hours to stabilize blood sugar. Drink 2-3L of water daily, adding electrolytes if you sweat.
  • D - Diary: Track attacks to identify patterns (weather, hormones, food) rather than guessing.
  • S - Stress: Use biofeedback or diaphragmatic breathing (4-7-8 method) to dampen the sympathetic nervous system.

Acute Medications (Abortive)

Acute medications are taken to stop an attack that has already started. The most common error is waiting too long; treating while pain is mild significantly increases success.

Triptans
GOLD STANDARD
Formulations
Tablets: Sumatriptan, Eletriptan, Rizatriptan.
ODT (Dissolvable): Rizatriptan (Maxalt-MLT) and Zolmitriptan (Zomig ZMT). These dissolve on the tongue but are still absorbed in the stomach; highly useful if you cannot swallow pills or need convenience.
Nasal Spray: Sumatriptan and Zolmitriptan. Faster onset (15 mins) and bypasses the stomach.
Injection: Sumatriptan SC. The fastest option (10 mins).
Why Use Non-Oral?
Migraines cause "gastric stasis" (stomach paralysis), meaning pills may sit in the stomach for hours without working. Nasal sprays and injections bypass the gut to work faster and are essential if you are vomiting.
Mechanism
Serotonin (5-HT1B/1D) agonists. They constrict dilated blood vessels and block the release of CGRP.
Side Effects
Flushing, tingling, and "triptan chest" (tightness in chest/throat). Contraindicated in patients with history of stroke, heart attack, or uncontrolled hypertension.
Cost
Low ($). Most are generic and covered by insurance ($10–$30/month).
Gepants (Oral CGRP Antagonists)
MODERN / CV SAFE
Common Options
Ubrogepant (Ubrelvy), Rimegepant (Nurtec ODT), Zavegepant (Zavzpret - Nasal).
Mechanism
Blocks the CGRP receptor directly without constricting blood vessels.
Efficacy
Effective for pain and associated symptoms (nausea, light sensitivity). Can be taken late in an attack and still work.
Side Effects
Very well tolerated. Mild nausea or dry mouth. No cardiovascular risk, making them safe for patients who cannot take Triptans.
Cost
High ($$$). Brand name only. Often requires prior authorization or manufacturer savings cards (~$0–$50 with card, >$900 retail).
Ditans
ALTERNATIVE
Common Options
Lasmiditan (Reyvow).
Mechanism
Selectively targets the 5-HT1F receptor on nerves (not blood vessels). No vasoconstriction.
Efficacy
Effective for pain freedom.
Side Effects
Significant dizziness and sedation. Driving restriction: You must not drive for 8 hours after taking a dose. Controlled substance (Schedule V).
Cost
High ($$$). Brand name only.
Ergots
RESCUE
Common Options
Dihydroergotamine (DHE) - Migranal, Trudhesa.
Mechanism
Broad spectrum serotonin agonist. Very potent vasoconstrictor.
Efficacy
Often used for "status migrainosus" (attacks lasting >3 days) or when triptans fail. Nasal spray or injection only.
Side Effects
Nausea is common (often given with anti-nausea meds). Strong vasoconstriction—strictly contraindicated in cardiovascular disease.
Cost
High ($$$). Trudhesa is brand name; DHE ampules vary.

Medication Overuse Headache (Rebound)

The "Vicious Cycle" of Treatment: Paradoxically, the medications used to treat migraines can cause migraines if used too frequently. This is the most common cause of chronic daily headache.

Medication Overuse Headache (MOH) occurs when acute painkillers are taken so often that the brain becomes sensitized, leading to a "rebound" headache as soon as the medication wears off. This traps the patient in a cycle of taking more medication to treat the pain caused by the medication itself.

  • The Limit: To prevent MOH, you must strictly limit acute medications (Triptans, Excedrin, NSAIDs, Opiates) to fewer than 10 days per month (or 2 days per week).
  • Why it happens: Frequent exposure to analgesics downregulates pain receptors, lowering your pain threshold.
  • Difficulty to Treat: MOH is notoriously difficult because the only cure is stopping the offending medication. This often leads to a temporary, but significant, worsening of headaches ("withdrawal") before improvement occurs.

Treatment Strategy

Managing MOH requires a "washout" period where acute medications are stopped. We often use a "bridge therapy" (such as a short course of steroids) to help you survive the withdrawal week while simultaneously starting a robust preventative medication to rebuild your threshold.

Preventative Medications

Indicated if you experience 4 or more migraine days per month or significant disability. These are taken daily (or injected monthly) to prevent attacks from starting.

CGRP Monoclonal Antibodies
TARGETED BIOLOGIC
Common Options
Erenumab (Aimovig), Fremanezumab (Ajovy), Galcanezumab (Emgality).
Mechanism
Injectable antibodies that neutralize CGRP or block its receptor. The only preventative designed specifically for migraine.
Efficacy
~50% of patients see a 50% reduction in migraine days. High adherence due to monthly dosing.
Side Effects
Injection site reactions. Aimovig specifically is associated with constipation.
Cost
High ($$$). Requires insurance approval, usually after failing 2 oral generics.
Oral Preventatives (Generic Standards)
FIRST LINE
Beta-Blockers
Propranolol, Metoprolol.
Pros: Good for anxiety/high BP.
Cons: Fatigue, exercise intolerance, depression.
Cost: Low ($).
Anticonvulsants
Topiramate (Topamax).
Pros: Effective, weight loss.
Cons: "Brain fog," tingling fingers, kidney stones, taste perversion (soda tastes flat).
Cost: Low ($).
Antidepressants
Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline.
Pros: Helps sleep and neck pain.
Cons: Dry mouth, weight gain, sedation.
Cost: Low ($).
Botox
PROCEDURAL
Indication
Chronic Migraine Only (15+ headache days/month). Not for episodic migraine.
Mechanism
31 micro-injections into head/neck muscles every 12 weeks. Blocks pain transmission from nerve endings.
Efficacy
Highly effective for chronic sufferers; often reduces severity significantly.
Side Effects
Neck pain, temporary eyebrow droop (rare).
Cost
High ($$$). Covered by insurance for chronic migraine.

Bioenergetic & Neuromodulation Devices

FDA-cleared neuromodulation devices use electrical or magnetic currents to alter nerve activity. These are excellent "non-drug" options for patients who cannot tolerate medications or want to avoid systemic side effects.

Cefaly (e-TNS)
DEVICE
What it is
External Trigeminal Nerve Stimulation. An electrode worn on the forehead.
Use
Acute: 60-min session to abort attack. Preventative: 20-min daily session.
Cost
~$379 (One-time purchase). Available OTC.
Nerivio (REN)
DEVICE
What it is
Remote Electrical Neuromodulation. A wearable armband controlled by smartphone.
Use
Worn for 45 mins at onset of migraine. Triggers "Conditioned Pain Modulation" (distracting the brain from head pain).
Cost
Requires prescription. Costs vary by insurance (~$50-$100 per 12 treatments).
gammaCore (nVNS)
DEVICE
What it is
Non-invasive Vagus Nerve Stimulator. Handheld device placed against the neck.
Use
Used twice daily for prevention or multiple times to abort acute attacks.
Cost
~$450 per 3-month supply (refillable card). Requires prescription.
SAVI Dual (SpringTMS)
DEVICE
What it is
Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation. Device held to the back of the head.
Use
Delivers a magnetic pulse to disrupt Cortical Spreading Depression (aura).
Cost
High rental fee; coverage varies.

Integrative Options & Supplements

These options raise the migraine threshold naturally. They often take 2–3 months of consistent use to show full benefit. We avoid Butterbur due to liver toxicity concerns.

Acute Integrative Rescue
SYMPTOM MANAGEMENT
Rest & Environment
Resting in a dark, quiet, and cool room is often essential to reduce sensory input (photophobia/phonophobia) and allow the nervous system to calm down.
Cold Therapy
Applying ice packs or a cool cloth to the forehead, temples, or back of the neck can numb pain and help constrict dilated blood vessels.
Hydration
Drinking water or electrolyte fluids at the onset of an attack can help, provided nausea is controlled enough to keep it down.
Ginger
HERBAL ACUTE
Rationale
Potent anti-inflammatory and anti-nausea properties. A 2014 double-blind study compared Ginger to Sumatriptan.
Evidence
Study Result: Ginger (250mg dried powder) was statistically as effective as Sumatriptan 50mg for achieving pain relief at 2 hours, with significantly fewer side effects.
Dose
500–1000mg dried ginger powder (capsule or tea) at onset.
Cost
Very Low ($).
Magnesium
ESSENTIAL
Rationale
Stabilizes nerve cell membranes; 50% of sufferers are deficient. Especially helpful for aura and menstrual migraine.
Dose/Form
400–600mg daily. Magnesium Glycinate is preferred for absorption and stomach tolerance. (Oxide often causes diarrhea).
Cost
Low (~$15–20/month).
Riboflavin (Vitamin B2)
MITOCHONDRIAL SUPPORT
Rationale
Boosts mitochondrial energy production in the brain. Level B evidence for efficacy.
Dose
400mg daily. Safe, but causes harmless bright yellow urine.
Cost
Low (~$15/month).
Coenzyme Q10
ENERGY
Rationale
Supports cellular energy metabolism. 48% of patients achieve >50% reduction in attacks.
Dose
100–300mg daily. Ubiquinol form is better absorbed than ubiquinone.
Cost
Moderate (~$30–50/month).
Non-Pharmacologic Therapies
LIFESTYLE
Biofeedback
Level A evidence. Trains you to warm your hands or relax muscles, reducing autonomic arousal.
Acupuncture
Proven superior to no treatment; effective for reducing frequency with fewer side effects than drugs.
Light Therapy
FL-41 Glasses: Rose-tinted lenses block painful blue light.
Green Light: Narrow-band green light exposure (1-2 hrs) can reduce headache intensity.
